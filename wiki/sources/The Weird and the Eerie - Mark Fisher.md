# The Weird and the Eerie — Mark Fisher (2016)

## Overview

A short work of critical theory arguing that the **weird** and the **eerie** are two distinct aesthetic modes, each with its own logic, affects, and cultural lineage. Fisher's central intervention is to disentangle both from Freud's *unheimlich* (uncanny), which has dominated the academic study of horror and the strange. Where the uncanny always folds the strange back into the domestic and psychological, the weird and the eerie move in the opposite direction: they let us see the inside from the perspective of the **outside**.

The book is split into two halves: a section on the weird (five chapters) and a section on the eerie (seven chapters). Fisher draws on fiction, film, television, and music across both.

---

## Core Distinctions

### Weird vs. Eerie vs. Uncanny

| Mode | Constituted by | Core affect | Typical terrain |
|---|---|---|---|
| Uncanny | Strange within the familiar | Dread, recognition | Domestic, familial |
| Weird | Presence of what does not belong | Fascination (jouissance) | Any world where the outside intrudes |
| Eerie | Failure of absence / failure of presence | Apprehension, serenity, allure | Emptied landscapes, ruins, deep history |

The uncanny always processes the outside through the gaps of the inside. The weird and the eerie make the opposite move.

---

## The Weird

**Definition:** A particular kind of perturbation — a sensation of *wrongness*. A weird entity or object should not exist here; its presence forces us to conclude that our conceptual frameworks are inadequate, not the entity itself.

**Key distinction from fantasy:** Fantasy is set in wholly separate worlds. The weird requires *egress* — an opening between this world and another. The outside intrudes into the familiar locale.

**Relationship to horror:** The weird is not primarily terrifying but *fascinating*. Lovecraft's characters are often terrified; his readers seldom are. Fascination is the shared affect.

**Key figures and works covered:**
- [[H.P. Lovecraft]] — foundational; practically invented the weird tale. Non-Euclidean geometries, hypernaturalism, the Cthulhu mythos as collective fictional system
- [[H.G. Wells]] — weird against the worldly; science as vector for the outside
- [[The Fall]] (Mark E. Smith) — grotesque and weird in post-punk form
- [[Tim Powers]] — time loops, fate, the weird's relationship to destiny
- [[Rainer Werner Fassbinder]] / [[Philip K. Dick]] — simulations and unworlding
- [[David Lynch]] (*Mulholland Drive*, *Inland Empire*) — curtains and holes; the weird as architecture of thresholds and ontological collapse

---

## The Eerie

**Definition:** Constituted by a *failure of absence* (something where there should be nothing) or a *failure of presence* (nothing where there should be something). The central enigma is always **agency**: is there an agent here? What kind? Is it acting intentionally?

**Key distinction from the weird:** The weird is constituted by presence; the eerie by the presence or absence of agency. The eerie clings to emptied or partially emptied spaces — landscapes, ruins, abandoned structures — more than to enclosed domestic spaces.

**Eerie calm:** The serenity associated with the eerie is a detachment from mundane urgencies, an opening onto forces ordinarily concealed.

**Political dimension:** Capital is an eerie entity — conjured from nothing, exerting force everywhere, never present in any substantial sense. The unconscious (Freud) is also eerie: a failure of presence that produces effects.

**Key figures and works covered:**

*Fiction:*
- [[Daphne du Maurier]] — "The Birds" (collective animal agency), "Don't Look Now" (fate as eerie agent; Nic Roeg adaptation)
- [[Christopher Priest]] — *The Affirmation*, *The Glamour*: gaps in identity and memory; negative hallucination; glamour as eerie invisibility
- [[Margaret Atwood]] — *Surfacing*: narrator as eerie enigma, outside apprehended from inside
- [[Joan Lindsay]] — *Picnic at Hanging Rock*: the eerie with a positive allure; the outside as liberation as much as peril

*Television:*
- [[M.R. James]] — "Oh, Whistle, and I'll Come to You, My Lad", "A Warning to the Curious": template for inorganic artefacts calling up ancient threat; East Anglian terrain as eerie landscape
- [[Nigel Kneale]] — *Quatermass and the Pit*, *The Stone Tape*, *Quatermass* (1979): scientific remotivation of the supernatural; Thanatos as impersonal pilot; stones as recording media; inorganic agency
- [[Alan Garner]] — *Red Shift*, *The Owl Service*: mythic time vs. linear time; landscape stained by traumatic repetition; fate operating through inorganic artefacts

*Film:*
- [[Jonathan Glazer]] — *Under the Skin*: alien among us; eerie reverse anthropology of capitalist culture; the human body as prosthesis
- [[Stanley Kubrick]] — *2001: A Space Odyssey* (monolith as paradigm eerie object), *The Shining* (hotel as recording system; forces of unknown nature and intent)
- [[Andrei Tarkovsky]] — *Solaris* (alien ocean; mismatching intelligences), *Stalker* (the Zone as eerie space without special effects; ethics of the eerie)
- [[Christopher Nolan]] — *Interstellar*: love as eerie agent; providence vs. indifference; the Tesseract and the time loop

*Music/Audio:*
- [[Brian Eno]] — *On Land* (1982): ambient music as sonic equivalent of eerie landscape; acousmatic sound; Suffolk coastal terrain; landscape as agent

## Key Theoretical Concepts

- **Failure of absence / failure of presence** — Fisher's core structural definition of the eerie
- **Agency** — the central question the eerie always poses
- **Eerie Thanatos** — transpersonal death drive operating through inorganic artefacts and landscape (Kneale, Garner)
- **Stone tape / inorganic recording** — matter as recording medium for traumatic events (Kneale, Negarestani)
- **Negative hallucination** — not seeing what is there; the eerie as constitutive gap in perception (Priest, Freud)
- **Phylogenetic memory** — trauma encoded in biological or geological strata (Kneale, Ballard)
- **Mythic time** — non-linear temporal structure in which past events are simultaneously present (Garner)
- **Acousmatic sound** — sound detached from visible source; intrinsically eerie (Eno)

## Notes

Fisher distinguishes the eerie from the merely mysterious: any mystery won't do — there must also be a sense of alterity, a feeling that the enigma involves forms of knowledge or agency beyond common experience. Once the mystery is resolved, the eerie dissipates.

Joan Lindsay's *Picnic at Hanging Rock* is given the final chapter as the clearest example of an eerie that is not coded as threatening or malign — the outside as a positive, liberatory dissolution rather than a fatal trap.
