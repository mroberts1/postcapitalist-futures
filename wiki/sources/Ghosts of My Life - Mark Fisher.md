# Ghosts of My Life — Mark Fisher (2014)

## Overview

A collection of Fisher's critical essays, organized into four thematic sections. The book's unifying thread is the concept of **hauntology** — borrowed from Derrida and repurposed to describe a cultural condition in which the future has been cancelled and the present is haunted by lost possibilities. The title essay, opening the collection, reads Goldie, Japan and Tricky as three moments in a single hauntological lineage running through British popular music.

---

## Part 1: Lost Futures

### 'Ghosts of My Life' (Goldie, Japan, Tricky)
The opening essay is Fisher's most personal and perhaps his finest piece of music criticism. It moves from Rufige Kru's 1993 darkside Jungle EP *Ghosts of My Life* (which samples David Sylvian) back to Japan's 1981 art-pop masterpiece 'Ghosts', then forward to Tricky's 1995 *Maxinquaye*.

Key arguments:
- **Jungle as sonic fiction**: darkside Jungle's identification with the android and the inhuman was a perverse utopianism — annihilation as the condition of the radically new. Goldie as cautionary tale: the moment he signed his work under a personal name rather than a collective alias, his music declined.
- **Japan's 'Ghosts'**: art pop as class anxiety — working-class autodidacts haunted by the fear that their transformation will always be incomplete, that "the rude spectres of Lewisham will return". Sylvian's singing voice as the faking of a fake (Bryan Ferry's pseudo-Geordie timelessness).
- **Tricky as hauntological medium**: writing from his dead mother's voice, inducing female singers into trance states, his music as haunted transmission rather than authored expression. *Maxinquaye* as purgatorial landscape. The hauntological crepitation of vinyl crackle (shared with Burial a decade later).

### 'The Slow Cancellation of the Future'
The theoretical centrepiece of the book. Opens with the final episode of *Sapphire and Steel* (1982) — two detectives of time, trapped in a café floating in deep space, frozen forever, time having stopped. Fisher uses this as a figure for the general cultural condition.

Drawing on Franco 'Bifo' Berardi's *After The Future*: the "slow cancellation of the future" began in the 1970s-80s with the collapse of the modernist assumption that time moves forward into genuine novelty. The result is a culture of **anachronism and inertia** disguised as perpetual change: constant surface novelty ("newness") concealing a deep temporal stasis.

Key argument: **popular music is the index**. In the 1960s and 70s, someone returning from a decade away would be shocked by what music sounded like. Now, someone returning from the 1990s would find 2010s music largely recognizable. The rate of genuine mutation has slowed to near-zero. This is not simply nostalgia but a structural condition: capital requires the cancellation of futures that might be different from the present.

**Hauntology as cultural diagnosis**: the ghost is not the past but the *future that never arrived* — the BBC Radiophonic Workshop's modernist promise, the welfare-state utopia of the postwar period, the sense that technology would produce genuine liberation rather than more sophisticated entertainment.

## Part 2: The Return of the 70s

Essays on Joy Division ('No Longer the Pleasures'), the Jimmy Savile scandal, *Life on Mars*, and David Peace. The 1970s as the decade capitalism won — and the decade culture keeps compulsively revisiting. Fisher reads this return not as nostalgia but as an attempt to work through the unresolved traumas of the moment when the future was foreclosed.

## Part 3: Hauntology

The longest and most substantial section. Extended essays on:

- **Burial** — 'London After the Rave' and 'Downcast Angel: Interview with Burial': Burial's music as sonic ghost of rave's collective euphoria, now degraded and dispersed into lonely post-urban spaces. The vinyl crackle as hauntological signature — not authenticity but the *trace* of something no longer present.
- **The Caretaker** — 'Memory Disorder: Interview with The Caretaker' and the sleeve notes to *Theoretically Pure Anterograde Amnesia*: ballroom music slowly consumed by static and degradation; memory as active forgetting rather than retrieval.
- **Ghost Box Records, Belbury Poly, The Focus Group** — 'Nostalgia for Modernism': hauntological music's attachment to a lost BBC public-service future, educational films, public information broadcasts. A modernism that feels like nostalgia for a future that was promised and not delivered.
- **John Foxx** — two interviews and an essay: Foxx as the precursor figure, the one who got there first but without the context to be fully understood.
- **James Blake, Kanye West, Drake, Darkstar** — 'Party Hauntology': the mainstreaming of hauntological affect into pop, R&B and grime. Whether this represents dilution or democratisation.
- **Little Axe** — 'Hauntological Blues': the blues as originary hauntological form.

## Part 4: The Stain of Place

Psychogeographical essays:
- **Chris Petit's *Content*** — 'Grey Area': motorway Britain, non-places, the eerie texture of late capitalist infrastructure.
- **Handsworth Songs** (Black Audio Film Collective) and the English riots.
- **Patrick Keiller's *Robinson in Ruins*** — 'Tremors of an Imperceptible Future': landscape as recording medium for suppressed histories.
- **Laura Oldfield Ford's *Savage Messiah*** — introduction by Fisher: psychogeography of the brutalist estate, the ruins of the post-war social democratic project.
- **Junior Boys' *So This Is Goodbye*** — 'Nomadalgia': homesickness for a place that never existed.
- **Christopher Nolan's *Inception*** — 'The Lost Unconscious': the film as symptom of capitalist realism's colonization of dreamwork.
- **Patience (After Sebald)** — Sebald and the stain of place; landscape haunted by what has been erased.

## Key Concepts

- **[[Lost Futures]]** — the book's central contribution; the slow cancellation of the future as cultural condition
- **[[Hauntology]]** — Fisher's full development of the concept through music and culture
- **Depressive hedonia** — developed further from *Capitalist Realism*
- **Non-places** (Augé) — generic transit spaces as symptom; already visible in *Sapphire and Steel*'s service station
- **Scenius** (Brian Eno) — collective intelligence of a scene, as opposed to individual genius; Goldie's decline when he left the Jungle scenius
