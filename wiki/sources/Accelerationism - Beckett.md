# Accelerationism: how a fringe philosophy predicted the future we live in

## Summary

A long-form Guardian feature by Andy Beckett tracing accelerationism from its fictional origins through the Warwick CCRU to its fracture into left and right variants. Published shortly after Mark Fisher's death in January 2017, it provides the most detailed journalistic account of the CCRU's culture and atmosphere — filling in biographical and institutional texture largely absent from Fisher's own published writing.

---

## Key Takeaways

### The Term's Origin
The word "accelerationist" was coined by Roger Zelazny in his 1967 science fiction novel *Lord of Light*, where it described a faction of revolutionaries pushing their society to a "higher level" through rapid technological transformation. Benjamin Noys borrowed it in 2010 as a critical label for the movement he was attacking.

### French Roots
Accelerationism traces to post-1968 French Marxism. Deleuze and Guattari's *Anti-Oedipus* (1972) suggested the left should "accelerate the process" of capitalism's liberating tendencies rather than simply oppose it. Lyotard's *Libidinal Economy* (1974) went further, arguing capitalism was even *enjoyed* by its subjects and that there was no alternative. Both books were controversial in France but became foundational texts for the Warwick group once translated into English (1983 and 1993 respectively).

### The CCRU at Warwick
Mark Fisher was Sadie Plant's former student at Birmingham before arriving at Warwick in the mid-1990s. Plant and Fisher were the technophile counterpart to Land's more nihilistic philosophical project — Plant had an early Apple Mac, Fisher was an early mobile phone user, both were invested in rave culture and jungle music as embodiments of accelerationist aesthetics.

The **CCRU** (Cybernetic Culture Research Unit) was founded in 1995. At its peak it operated from a single office in the Warwick philosophy department, then decamped to a rented room above a Body Shop in Leamington Spa. Members describe the collective as a near-total absorption: "There was almost no disharmony. There was no leisure. We tried not to be apart from each other... When everyone is keeping up with everyone else, the collective element increased is *speed*" (Iain Hamilton Grant).

The **Virtual Futures conferences** (1994, 1995, 1996) attracted philosophers, science fiction fans, natural scientists, and cultural trend-spotters (including a writer from *The Face*). Nick Land gave notorious performances — in one, lying on the floor croaking into a microphone while Robin Mackay played jungle records behind him.

The CCRU published a "conveyor belt of cryptic articles, crammed with invented terms, sometimes speculative to the point of being fiction." Land himself was living in his office with Pot Noodles, washing clothes in the staff toilets, fuelled by skunk and amphetamine.

### Collapse
In 1997 Plant resigned, citing the CCRU's "charged personal, political and philosophical dynamics." In 1998 Land resigned too. The remaining members moved to the Leamington Spa room, drifting from accelerationism into occultism, numerology, Lovecraft, and Aleister Crowley (who was born in Leamington). Land drew occult diagrams on the walls; multiple members suffered mental and physical crises. Land himself, after "perhaps a year of fanatical abuse" of amphetamine and "prolonged artificial insomnia," broke down in the early 2000s and disappeared from public view.

### Fisher Post-CCRU
Fisher's k-punk blog (from 2003) retained some Warwick traits — quoting Deleuze and Guattari — but shed the CCRU's aggressive rhetoric and pro-capitalist politics. Nick Srnicek encountered Fisher through k-punk; they became friends and collaborators. Fisher's increasing impatience with both capitalism's stasis and the left's failure to engage with new technology fed into the left accelerationist turn of the 2010s.

The article notes that towards the end of his life Fisher was "increasingly preoccupied by the idea that Britain was not heading towards some great leap forward, but stasis" — the reverse of acceleration, linking directly to his work on [[Hauntology]] and [[Lost Futures]].

### Left vs Right Accelerationism
Srnicek and Williams' 2013 *Manifesto for an Accelerationist Politics* and *Inventing the Future* (2015) represent the L/ACC turn. Srnicek told Beckett they had "given up on the term" by 2017. Land's post-CCRU trajectory went the other way: through neoreaction (NRx) and "The Dark Enlightenment" (2013) to sympathy for Trump and Brexit as accelerating forces.
