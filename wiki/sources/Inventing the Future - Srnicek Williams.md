# Inventing the Future: Postcapitalism and a World Without Work

**Authors:** Nick Srnicek and Alex Williams
**Publisher:** Verso, 2015
**ISBN:** 978-1-78478-096-8

---

## Overview

A manifesto-style work of left political theory that diagnoses the failure of contemporary left politics through the concept of **[[Folk Politics]]** and proposes an alternative: a counter-hegemonic programme organised around a **[[Post-Work]]** platform. The book sits at the intersection of accelerationist thought, Laclau/Mouffe-style hegemony theory, and postcapitalist political economy.

The central provocation: the left has lost not because it lacks moral righteousness or commitment, but because it has unconsciously adopted a set of strategic assumptions — folk politics — that are structurally incapable of challenging global capitalism. The book's two halves move from diagnosis (what is wrong with the contemporary left) to prescription (what a viable left alternative would look like).

---

## Part One: Diagnosis

### The Folk-Political Common Sense (Ch.1–2)

[[Folk Politics]] names a historically sedimented set of strategic intuitions that dominate the contemporary left: a preference for the immediate over the abstract, the local over the global, the particular over the universal, the spontaneous over the institutional. Manifest in Occupy, horizontalism, anarchist direct action, the Zapatistas, slow-food localism, and ethical consumerism.

Three axes of immediacy:
- **Temporal**: reactive rather than strategic; single-issue; ephemeral (occupations, TAZs); nostalgic (return to Keynesianism)
- **Spatial**: local over global; small over large; exit over hegemony; direct action
- **Conceptual**: everyday over structural; feeling over thinking; particular over universal; ethical over political

Folk politics is not simply wrong — it can achieve local successes and is "necessary but insufficient." The problem is treating it as *sufficient* for the project of transcending capitalism. Folk-political approaches cannot scale, cannot build lasting counter-power, and cannot compete with capitalism's universalism.

Three explanations for folk politics' rise:
1. **Response to complexity**: capitalism is too abstract to grasp directly, so politics retreats to what feels tangible and human-scale
2. **Historical reaction**: to the failures of state communism (Leninism) and the exclusions of old-left labour movements
3. **Disillusionment with party politics**: post-political consensus made electoral change feel impossible

### Why Neoliberalism Won (Ch.3)

Neoliberalism's rise was not inevitable but was a *political achievement* — built through patient decades of institutional construction: think tanks (Mont Pèlerin Society, IEA, Heritage Foundation), university departments, networks of influence that eventually captured the common sense. While the left retreated into folk-political resistance, the right built hegemony.

Key neoliberal tactics: defeat of organised labour (breaking union power through physical confrontation, legislation, supply-chain disaggregation, "negative solidarity"); co-optation of social democratic parties; hollowing out of electoral alternatives.

## Part Two: Alternative

### Left Modernity (Ch.4)

Three philosophical pillars for a left alternative to folk politics:

**Hyperstitional Progress**: Progress is not a pre-given trajectory of history but a *hyperstition* — a fiction that aims to make itself true by catalysing dispersed sentiment into historical force. Term borrowed from CCRU. Progress is politically constructed, not guaranteed; but the left must articulate enticing visions of the future rather than ceding futurity to neoliberalism. Contra postmodern abandonment of grand narratives (Lyotard), the left must reclaim modernity as a contested field.

**Subversive Universalism**: Capitalism is an expansionary universal; particularisms cannot compete with it. The left needs a counter-universal — but not the Eurocentric homogenising universalism of colonial modernity. Instead, universals are empty placeholders that hegemonic particulars temporarily occupy. They are always incomplete, always open to contestation from below ("universalism from below"). The universal functions as a subversive vector: feminist, anti-racist and anti-colonial movements have consistently used universal claims to expose the particularity of existing "universals."

**Synthetic Freedom**: Against capitalism's negative freedom (formal absence of interference), synthetic freedom names the *constructed* capacity to act: built through income, time, education, collective resources, and technological development. Freedom without material means is formal and hollow. Key implication: the provision of basic goods, free time, and a [[Universal Basic Income]] are not welfare measures but *freedom-expanding* political demands.

### The Future Isn't Working (Ch.5)

Analysis of contemporary labour: automation is displacing jobs across the economy; the result is not simply unemployment but labour market polarisation between high-skill cognitive work and low-wage service work. A new wave of automation (machine learning, robotics, algorithmic management) threatens both poles. The standard response — defend jobs, retrain workers — is inadequate. The left should instead embrace automation as a political demand rather than treat it as a threat.

Also analyses the "surplus population" — those structurally excluded from wage labour — as a latent political force.

### Post-Work Imaginaries (Ch.6)

Four integrated demands for a **[[Post-Work]]** platform, presented as "non-reformist reforms":

1. **[[Full Automation]]**: Enthusiastically accelerate automation as a political project. Between 47–80% of current jobs technically automatable. Full automation is a utopian demand to reduce necessary labour as much as possible. Barriers: technical (tacit/affective/care work), economic (cheap labour depresses investment), moral (some care work should remain human). All addressable through policy and politics.

2. **Shorter Working Week**: Reduce to a three-day weekend with no pay cut. Historically, working week fell from 60 to 35 hours between 1900 and the Depression — then stabilised. Reduction simultaneously responds to automation (redistributes work), cuts carbon footprint, improves health, and — crucially — builds class power by tightening the labour market.

3. **[[Universal Basic Income]]**: Unconditional income for all, supplementing (not replacing) the welfare state. Must be sufficient to live on; universal (no means-testing); additive to public services. Transforms the labour/capital power relation by making wage labour truly voluntary, tightening the labour market, enabling strikes, and recognising unwaged reproductive labour. Explicitly feminist demand: enables exit from dysfunctional relationships, experimentation with family forms.

4. **Cultural shift away from the work ethic**: The work ethic is the deepest political obstacle. Ideologically, suffering is treated as the precondition of deserving. Counter-hegemonic cultural work must denaturalise work as the source of identity and meaning, tapping into the already-widespread hatred of jobs (only 13% globally report finding their work engaging).

The four demands form a positive feedback loop: automation amplifies the case for UBI and shorter hours; shorter hours tighten the labour market and incentivise automation; UBI amplifies class power and frees time.

### Building Power (Ch.7–8)

The left must rebuild counter-hegemonic infrastructure: think tanks, media, educational institutions, political platforms. Drawing on Laclau/Mouffe: constructing a populist "people" through equivalential chains of demands. Two strategic tools: *plumbing* (building durable institutions and platforms) and *ignition* (moments of mobilisation that energise a coalition). Neither alone is sufficient.

## Critical Notes

- Critics from the left argue the book underplays the difficulty of building counter-hegemonic institutions without a class base, and that the UBI demand has been captured by libertarian and tech-billionaire discourse
- The folk politics concept can read as dismissive of actually-existing movement victories (civil rights, feminism) that used exactly the tactics it critiques
- The book's accelerationist heritage leads some to worry about uncritical technophilia; the authors insist automation must be politically directed, but the mechanism for this remains underdeveloped
- Published 2015, pre-Trump/Brexit: some of the populism analysis reads differently after the right successfully built its own counter-hegemonic populism
