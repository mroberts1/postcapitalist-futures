# K-Punk: The Collected and Unpublished Writings — Mark Fisher (2018)

## Overview

Posthumous anthology of Fisher's writing from his k-punk blog (2003–2016), selected and edited by Darren Ambrose with a foreword by Simon Reynolds. Seven parts covering books, film/TV, music, politics, interviews, reflections, and the unfinished *Acid Communism* introduction. The collection runs to approximately 800 pages.

The k-punk blog (`k-punk.abstractdynamics.org`) was one of the most significant critical writing projects of the 2000s — part of a wider blogosphere (including writers like Robin Mackay/Reza Negarestani at Hyperstition, and Ian Penman) that fused continental philosophy with music and culture criticism in a register that was neither academic nor journalistic.

---

## Structure

### Part One: Methods of Dreaming — Books
Essays on J.G. Ballard (multiple pieces), Margaret Atwood, Patricia Highsmith's Ripley novels, and a Zer0 Books manifesto. Ballard is the presiding figure: his work as a guide to the psychopathology of consumer capitalism. The Zer0 Books statement articulates the project of "intellectual without being academic, popular without being populist."

### Part Two: Screens, Dreams and Spectres — Film and Television
Criticism of Kubrick, Cronenberg (*eXistenZ*), Christopher Nolan (*Batman Begins*, *Inception*, *The Box*), *Doctor Who*, *The Prisoner*, *Sapphire and Steel*, *Avatar*, *The Shining*, and others. A sustained argument that certain works of popular culture think more rigorously about capitalism, subjectivity and power than most academic philosophy.

### Part Three: Choose Your Weapons — Writing on Music
The largest section. Covers Joy Division/Ian Curtis, The Fall (Mark E. Smith as abject genius of the weird), Burial, Demdike Stare, Rihanna, UK Funky, post-dubstep, and many others. The sustained theoretical argument: popular music as a site of genuine political-philosophical thought, not despite but because of its mass character.

### Part Four: For Now, Our Desire is Nameless — Political Writings
Essays on precarity, the left, anti-capitalism, consciousness-raising, and Fisher's evolving political positions. Includes 'Exiting the Vampire Castle' (2013) — his controversial critique of left identity politics and "call-out culture", arguing that personal virtue politics has replaced structural analysis and blocked the emergence of a genuine collective political subject.

### Part Five: We Have to Invent the Future — Interviews
Interviews covering *Capitalist Realism*, hauntology, lost futures, the left's failures, and Fisher's political vision. The title phrase encapsulates his orientation: not a return to an imagined past but the construction of genuinely new possibilities.

### Part Six: We Are Not Here to Entertain You — Reflections
More personal and polemical essays. Includes pieces on depression, on the experience of teaching, on the politics of mental health.

### Part Seven: Acid Communism
The unfinished introduction to what would have been Fisher's final book. See [[Acid Communism - Mark Fisher]] for a full summary.

## Key Themes Across the Collection

- **Postpunk as theory**: Joy Division, The Fall, Public Image Ltd. as thinkers of late capitalism's psychic effects
- **Hauntology**: developed across many music pieces before *Ghosts of My Life* collected them
- **The weird**: prefigures *The Weird and the Eerie* in readings of Ballard, Cronenberg, Kafka
- **Class and culture**: running concern with how class shapes cultural access, aspiration, and escape
- **Exiting the Vampire Castle**: the most controversial piece; sparked significant debate about the left's capacity for collective action vs. identitarian policing
