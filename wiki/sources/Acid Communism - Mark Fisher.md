# Acid Communism (Unfinished Introduction) — Mark Fisher (2018)

## Overview

The unfinished introduction to what would have been Fisher's final book, published posthumously in *K-Punk* (2018). It is the most concentrated statement of the political project Fisher was developing in his final years: a recovery of the emancipatory potential briefly visible in the 1960s-70s counterculture, theorized through Marcuse and directed against capitalist realism.

The piece was left incomplete at Fisher's death in January 2017. What exists is roughly 8,000 words covering the theoretical framing and the argument about the 1960s-70s; the main body of the book, which would have analyzed specific cultural texts and movements, was never written.

---

## Central Claim

The last forty years have been a project of **exorcising the spectre of a world which could be free**. Neoliberalism's real target was not its official enemies — Soviet communism or Keynesian social democracy, which were already collapsing under their own weight — but the experiments in democratic socialism and libertarian communism that were efflorescing at the end of the 1960s and beginning of the 1970s. The Pinochet coup in Chile (1973), Fisher argues, is the founding event of capitalist realism: the violent termination of Allende's democratic socialism, which demonstrated the lengths capital would go to make itself appear the only realistic option.

The installation of capitalist realism was therefore not inevitable but **achieved** — through inducements, seductions, and where necessary, repression. The ultimate effect was the extirpation of even the *idea* of democratic socialism or libertarian communism.

---

## Marcuse as Key Resource

Fisher rehabilitates Herbert Marcuse, whose work has long been out of print and out of fashion. Two books are central:

**Eros and Civilisation**: Marcuse's argument that capitalism requires a surplus repression of human libido beyond what civilization actually needs — the artificial creation of scarcity (especially *scarcity of time*) to keep people chained to unnecessary labour. The prospect of liberation — a life freed from drudgery — was always more immanent than capital could allow.

**One-Dimensional Man**: Art as the **Great Refusal** — "the protest against that which is." Art was a positive alienation, a rational negation of the existing order. The counterculture was partly animated by this: identification with figures who refused the work ethic, who claimed the right to idle and dream. Marcuse had already anticipated the counterculture's failure: the absorption of art into commercial space neutralizes its incompatibility with capitalist culture. The gangster, the beatnik, the vamp — converted from "images of another way of life" into "freaks or types of the same life."

The declining influence of Marcuse's *Eros and Civilisation* (out of print; *One-Dimensional Man* has remained a reference point) is itself evidence of capitalist realism's success.

## Acid Communism

The concept is a **provocation and a promise**: "a joke of sorts, but one with very serious purpose." It names the convergence, once glimpsed and now suppressed, of:
- **Class consciousness** — the organized demand for economic equality
- **Socialist-feminist consciousness-raising** — the expansion of political analysis into personal life and subjectivity
- **Psychedelic consciousness** — the transformation of perception and the dissolution of the ego's acquisitive attachments

These three currents ran together briefly in the late 1960s and early 1970s before being forcibly separated. Acid communism is the name for their potential fusion: "the fusion of new social movements with a communist project, an unprecedented aestheticisation of everyday life."

The concept is both historical (referring to actual tendencies) and **virtual** (a potential that was never actualized but continues to exert pressure). Potentials, Fisher insists, are real even when unrealized. The impress of "a world which could be free" can be detected in the very structures of the capitalist realist world that works to prevent it.

## The 1960s Revisited

Fisher begins his account with a deliberately modest example: Danny Baker's memoir of July 1966, listening to "River Deep Mountain High" and then "Bus Stop" on a transistor radio on a family canal holiday. The scene captures something very specific to that moment: existential security (a working-class family taking a holiday), genuine formal novelty in popular music, and a sense of "exorbitant sufficiency" — the present as enough, as its own complete world.

This is not nostalgia but political analysis: such moments were made possible by specific structural conditions (full employment, welfare state, cheap housing, public service broadcasting) that neoliberalism has systematically destroyed — and then told us were economically impossible.

**The Sixties haunt the current moment** not because of some unrepeatable confluence of factors but because the potentials they materialized — a life freed from drudgery, collective rather than individual forms of social life — have to be *continually suppressed*. "To recall these multiple forms of collectivity is less an act of remembering than of **unforgetting**, a counter-exorcism of the spectre of a world which could be free."

---

## The 1970s as Contested Terrain

Fisher insists the 1970s matters more than the 1960s for political reclamation. Reactionary narratives have cast the 1970s as the inevitable unraveling of Sixties excess — the hangover that made Thatcherism necessary. Recent historical work (Andy Beckett, Jefferson Cowie, John Medhurst) shows the opposite: the 1970s was a decade of struggle and transition in which emancipatory tendencies from the 1960s intensified. "For many politicised Britons, the decade was *not* the hangover after the Sixties; it was the point when the great Sixties party actually started."

Neoliberalism was therefore not an irresistible economic force but a **Leninist counter-revolution** — using think-tanks as intellectual vanguard, deploying bravura intelligence and ferocious energy to win a cultural battle. The left's failure to understand this has led to a politics of reaction and restoration rather than construction.

## Key Concepts

- **[[Acid Communism]]** — the spectre of a world which could be free
- **Surplus repression** (Marcuse) — capitalist imposition of unnecessary labour and scarcity
- **Great Refusal** (Marcuse) — art's inherent incompatibility with capitalist realism
- **Consciousness-raising** — socialist-feminist method of politicizing personal experience
- **Unforgetting** — Fisher's term for political reclamation of suppressed possibilities
- **Exorbitant sufficiency** — the quality of certain 1960s cultural moments: the present as enough
