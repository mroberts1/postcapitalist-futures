# Capitalist Realism: Is There No Alternative? — Mark Fisher (2009)

## Overview

Fisher's first and most influential book. A short (~80 page) polemical work arguing that capitalism has so thoroughly saturated the cultural atmosphere that it no longer presents itself as one option among many but as the ground of reality itself. The subtitle "Is There No Alternative?" inverts Thatcher's TINA doctrine into a genuine question Fisher sets out to answer.

The touchstone is Alfonso Cuarón's *Children of Men* (2006): a film that imagines the collapse of humanity but not of capitalism — its bureaucracies and consumer brands persist in a world where women have become infertile. This, Fisher argues, is not dystopian exaggeration but an accurate picture of our ideological condition: **it is easier to imagine the end of the world than the end of capitalism**.

---

## Core Argument

**Capitalist realism** is not a propaganda system but an *atmosphere* — a pervasive sense that capitalism is coextensive with reality itself, that alternatives are not merely undesirable but literally unthinkable. It does not operate through explicit advocacy but through the naturalization of its assumptions: that everything should be run as a business, that efficiency and market logic are facts rather than values, that individual psychology rather than structural conditions explains suffering.

## Chapter by Chapter

### 1. It's Easier to Imagine the End of the World
Establishes the core claim via *Children of Men* and the Jameson/Žižek formulation. Capitalist realism succeeds not despite anti-capitalist culture (gangster rap, corporate villainy in Hollywood) but partly *through* it — gestures of resistance are absorbed and neutralized.

### 2. What if You Held a Protest and Everyone Came?
Analyzes the failure of anti-capitalism as political force. **Interpassivity**: *Wall-E* performs our anti-capitalism for us, allowing guilt-free consumption. **Liberal communism** (Soros, Bono, Product Red) is not a corrective to capitalism but its dominant ideology — combining rapacious accumulation with ethical branding. Live 8 as the purest form: a protest everyone could agree with, demanding nothing structural.

### 3. Capitalism and the Real
Theoretical core. Uses the Lacanian distinction between the **Real** (what reality must suppress to constitute itself) and reality. Capitalist realism presents itself as reality while suppressing its own contradictions. Two key **aporias** capitalism cannot resolve without unraveling:

1. **Mental health**: Depression is the most treated condition in the NHS, afflicting people younger and younger. Rather than treating this as political — as the systemic effect of *privatized stress* — capitalism individualizes it as neurochemical. The "mental health plague" is evidence that capitalism is *dysfunctional*, not efficient.

2. **Bureaucracy**: Neoliberalism promised to eliminate bureaucracy but has massively proliferated it in new forms — "aims and objectives", targets, auditing, self-assessment. **Market Stalinism**: valuing the *representation* of achievement over achievement itself, just as Stalin's White Sea Canal was a triumph of publicity that could carry no cargo.

### 4. Reflexive Impotence, Immobilization and Liberal Communism
British students are not apathetic but **reflexively impotent**: they know things are bad but believe nothing can be done, and this belief is self-fulfilling. **Depressive hedonia**: not the inability to feel pleasure but the inability to do *anything else* — trapped in a loop of low-intensity gratification (social media, fast food, gaming) that forecloses engagement with difficulty.

Deleuze's **control society** vs. Foucault's disciplinary society: discipline operated through enclosed spaces (factory, school, prison); control operates through debt, perpetual assessment, and internalized surveillance. The debtor-addict is the figure of control, as the worker-prisoner was the figure of discipline.

### 5. October 6, 1979: 'Don't Let Yourself Get Attached to Anything'
Joy Division, Factory Records, and the cultural politics of post-punk. The date is Ian Curtis's first epileptic seizure. Fisher reads post-punk as an attempt to process the transition from Fordism to post-Fordism — Joy Division's music as the sonic texture of deindustrialisation.

### 6. All That Is Solid Melts into PR: Market Stalinism and Bureaucratic Anti-production
The new bureaucracy of post-Fordism: workers become their own auditors. The **big Other** (Žižek/Lacan): the collective fiction that social life requires — bureaucracy functions as its earthly representative. Kafka as prophet of distributed cybernetic power: the quest for a final authority can never end because the big Other cannot be encountered directly. Capitalist realism repeats the Stalinist fetish for symbolic achievement over real achievement.

### 7. Capitalist Realism as Dreamwork and Memory Disorder
Fredric Jameson on postmodernism and the atrophy of historical time. Capitalist realism as "dreamwork": it doesn't need to be believed, only inhabited.

### 8. 'There's No Central Exchange'
Post-Fordism as a form of distributed power that has no single command point to attack — which makes traditional oppositional politics structurally inadequate.

### 9. Marxist Supernanny
The failure of the **Father function** in late capitalism: the paternal concept of duty has been dissolved into the maternal imperative to enjoy. *Supernanny* as symptom. Fisher's proposal: a **paternalism without the Father** — Spinozist understanding of real interests vs. immediate desires. The left should not capitulate to neoliberal notions of freedom-as-consumption but insist on a concept of the general will and public space. Ends with the 2008 credit crisis as an opportunity: "The long, dark night of the end of history has to be grasped as an enormous opportunity."

## Key Concepts

- **[[Capitalist Realism]]** — the book's central concept; capitalism as naturalized atmosphere
- **Interpassivity** — cultural products performing critique on our behalf (Pfaller/Žižek)
- **Liberal communism** — Žižek's term for Bono/Soros-style ethical capitalism
- **Market Stalinism** — neoliberal bureaucracy valuing PR over substance
- **Reflexive impotence** — knowing things are bad but believing change is impossible
- **Depressive hedonia** — inability to do anything other than pursue low-intensity pleasure
- **Business ontology** — the assumption that everything should be run as a business
- **Privatization of stress** — treating structural social problems as individual neurological failures
- **Control society** — Deleuze's term for post-disciplinary power via debt and internalized surveillance

---

## Theoretical Resources

Fisher draws primarily on: Žižek (Lacan, ideology), Deleuze (control societies), Jameson (postmodernism), Foucault (discipline/biopolitics), Badiou (event, fidelity), Kafka (bureaucracy), and Spinoza (affects, real interests).
