# Universal Basic Income

## Definition

An unconditional cash payment made to every member of society regardless of employment status, income level, or behaviour. Three criteria distinguish a genuine UBI from conservative variants: (1) it must be **sufficient to live on**; (2) it must be **universal** — paid to all with no means-testing; (3) it must **supplement** the welfare state rather than replace it.

---

## Political Logic

In Srnicek and Williams's framework (*[[Inventing the Future - Srnicek Williams|Inventing the Future]]*), UBI's significance is primarily *political* rather than redistributive. Its key effects:

**Transforms the labour/capital power relation**: The proletariat is defined by its separation from the means of subsistence — it must sell its labour to survive. UBI partially undoes this structural dependency, making wage labour genuinely voluntary rather than coerced. This simultaneously increases class power (labour market tightens, firing loses disciplinary force, strikes become easier) and decommodifies labour.

**Converts precarity into voluntary flexibility**: Flexible and precarious work was initially *demanded by workers* (against the monotony of Fordist employment) before being co-opted by capital as a form of exploitation. UBI transforms precarity back into the freedom of flexible self-organisation.

**Recognises reproductive and care labour**: Like the 1970s "Wages for Housework" demand (Federici), UBI acknowledges that unwaged labour — childcare, eldercare, domestic work — contributes to social reproduction and deserves recognition. Unlike wages for housework, however, UBI does not lock workers into the wage relation but aims to move beyond it.

**Feminist demand**: Provides the financial independence necessary for synthetic freedom for women — enabling exit from dysfunctional relationships, alternative family arrangements, and genuine economic autonomy.

**Positive feedback with automation**: As UBI raises effective wages and reduces workers' desperation, employers face higher labour costs and greater incentive to automate. UBI thus amplifies the political project of [[Full Automation]].

## Historical Precedents

UBI has repeatedly appeared in political history and been nearly implemented:

- **1940s UK**: Thomas Paine's basic income idea; considered as alternative to the Beveridge Report
- **1960s–70s USA**: Over 1,300 economists petitioned Congress; Nixon and Carter both attempted legislation; pilot programmes in Canada and the United States; nearly enacted
- **Alaska Permanent Fund** (1982–present): Oil dividend paid to all residents — the one successful real-world implementation
- **Swiss referendum** (2016): Defeated but placed UBI on mainstream agenda
- **Namibia and India pilots** (2000s–2010s): Positive results on poverty, health, and entrepreneurship
- **Contemporary resurgence**: Krugman, Martin Wolf, *Financial Times*, *Economist* — mainstream economic press treating it as serious policy in response to automation

## The Conservative Trap

UBI is contested terrain. The libertarian/tech-billionaire variant — a single lump-sum replacing all welfare programmes — is a vector for *marketisation*, not emancipation. Milton Friedman's "negative income tax" and similar proposals use basic income to justify eliminating healthcare, housing, and education as public goods, forcing everything into the market. This must be actively resisted. The left demand for UBI is always: supplement, never replace.

---

## Funding

Standard objection ("where does the money come from?") is addressable through combinations of: progressive income and wealth taxation, inheritance taxes, carbon taxes, financial transaction taxes, reduction of redundant welfare bureaucracy, cutting military spending, cracking down on tax evasion. Research consistently suggests the net cost is manageable.
