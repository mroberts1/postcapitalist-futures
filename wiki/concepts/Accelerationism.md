# Accelerationism

## Definition

The political-philosophical thesis that the contradictions of capitalism should be *accelerated* rather than opposed or reformed — that pushing capitalist processes to their limit will either force a revolutionary rupture or unlock emancipatory potentials that existing left politics cannot access. The term covers a significant range of positions, from the nihilistic right to the programmatic left, united only by their rejection of decelerationist or reformist strategies.

Fisher engaged with accelerationism throughout his career, from his early involvement in the Cybernetic Culture Research Unit (CCRU) through to his final course, where it appeared as Lecture 11 of [[Postcapitalist Desire - Mark Fisher]].

## CCRU History

### Origins of the Term
The word "accelerationist" was coined by Roger Zelazny in his 1967 science fiction novel *Lord of Light*, where it named a revolutionary faction pushing their society to a "higher level" through rapid technological transformation. The critic Benjamin Noys borrowed it in 2010 as a label for the philosophical tendency he was attacking; it has been in regular use since.

### French Roots
Accelerationism proper begins in post-1968 French Marxism. Deleuze and Guattari's *Anti-Oedipus* (1972) argued that rather than simply opposing capitalism, the left should acknowledge its liberating tendencies and seek "to go still further... in the movement of the market... to 'accelerate the process'." Lyotard's *Libidinal Economy* (1974) went further, claiming capitalism was even *enjoyed* by those it reordered, and that there was no alternative. Both books were controversial in France — Lyotard later disowned *Libidinal Economy* as his "evil book" — but became foundational texts at Warwick once translated (1983 and 1993 respectively).

### Warwick and the CCRU
The British reception of these texts centred on Warwick University's philosophy department in the early 1990s, where Nick Land was a young lecturer. Land was slight and fragile-looking but intellectually devastating: "I've never witnessed anyone who could so forensically destroy a thesis" (Iain Hamilton Grant). By 1992 he was writing that capitalism had never been properly unleashed and that civilisation was "accelerating towards an apocalypse."

Fisher arrived at Warwick as Sadie Plant's former student from Birmingham. Fisher and Plant were the technophile counterpart to Land's nihilism — Plant had an early Apple Mac, Fisher was an early mobile phone user, both were invested in jungle and rave culture as aesthetic embodiments of accelerationist dynamics.

In 1995, Land, Plant, Fisher, Robin Mackay, Steve Goodman, and roughly two dozen others founded the **Cybernetic Culture Research Unit (CCRU)**. It operated first from a single cramped office in the Warwick philosophy department, later from a rented room above a Body Shop in Leamington Spa. Members describe a near-total collective absorption: "There was almost no disharmony. There was no leisure... When everyone is keeping up with everyone else, the collective element increased is *speed*" (Grant). Land by this point was living in his office, fuelled by skunk and amphetamine, with a tower of Pot Noodles on the desk.

The CCRU published a stream of cryptic articles — "crammed with invented terms, sometimes speculative to the point of being fiction" — and organised the **Virtual Futures conferences** (1994–96) at Warwick, drawing philosophers, science fiction fans, and cultural press. Land's performances were notorious: in one he lay on the floor croaking into a microphone while Mackay played jungle records.

### Collapse
In 1997 Plant resigned, finding the dynamics "irresistible to many, but I felt stifled." In 1998 Land followed. The remaining group withdrew to the Leamington Spa room, drifting from accelerationism into occultism, numerology, Lovecraft, and Crowley (who was born in Leamington). Land drew occult diagrams on the walls; multiple members suffered mental and physical crises. After what he later described as "perhaps a year of fanatical abuse" of amphetamine and "prolonged artificial insomnia," Land broke down in the early 2000s and vanished from public view. He eventually resurfaced in Shanghai, where he still lives.

## The CCRU and Nick Land's Theory

Fisher was a founding member of the **Cybernetic Culture Research Unit** (CCRU) at Warwick in the mid-1990s, alongside Nick Land, Sadie Plant, and others. The CCRU developed an extreme version of accelerationism drawing on Deleuze and Guattari's concept of deterritorialization, cybernetics, and science fiction: capital as a self-organizing, self-accelerating intelligence that was progressively dismantling all human institutions in favor of pure machinic process.

Nick Land's "Machinic Desire" (1993, collected in *Fanged Noumena*, 2012) — assigned in Lecture 11 of [[Postcapitalist Desire - Mark Fisher]] — is the key text. Land argues that capitalism is not a system controlled by humans but a destabilizing inhuman force that uses humans as vehicles: "the machinic unconscious" pursuing its own ends. The proper political response is not resistance but intensification — feeding the machine more, faster, until it breaks through into something beyond.

Fisher eventually broke with Land's position — which he came to see as a form of **capitalist realism in accelerated form**: accepting capital's logic as destiny while aestheticizing the destruction. Land's later political trajectory (toward neoreaction and "The Dark Enlightenment," 2013, a founding document of the NRx movement) confirmed for Fisher that right accelerationism had no emancipatory content.

## Left Accelerationism (L/ACC)

Fisher's pairing in Lecture 11 — Jameson alongside Land — signals his interest in a *left* accelerationism that retains the critique of decelerationism without Land's nihilism.

**Fredric Jameson**, "Utopia as Replication" (*Valences of the Dialectic*, 2009): Jameson argues that Walmart's logistical infrastructure — its planetary supply chain, its capacity to coordinate production and distribution at scale — contains within it, in distorted form, a utopian content: a demonstration that collective organization of needs is possible. The task is not to destroy capitalist infrastructure but to appropriate and redirect it.

**Nick Srnicek and Alex Williams**, *Inventing the Future* (2015) — assigned in Lecture 1 — develop a programmatic left accelerationism: embrace automation, demand universal basic income, reclaim technology for collective ends. Their "post-work" politics argues that the left should not defend existing labour arrangements (decelerationism) but accelerate automation in order to eliminate drudge work and free human time.

## Fisher's Position

Fisher's relationship to accelerationism was ambivalent and evolving. His clearest position:

- He rejected **right accelerationism** (Land) as a capitalist realist trap: celebrating capital's destructive force is not a politics but an aesthetics of defeat
- He was interested in **left accelerationist** impulses (Jameson, Srnicek/Williams) as part of the postcapitalist horizon
- He was sceptical of purely technological solutions — the emancipatory potential of automation depends entirely on *who controls it* and *what social forms surround it*
- His own contribution to accelerationist thought was **cultural**: the argument (in [[Acid Communism]]) that the counterculture's psychedelic and libidinal energies were a form of acceleration toward forms of life beyond work — and that neoliberalism's counter-revolution was precisely an effort to *decelerate* those tendencies

The CCRU period surfaces in Fisher's work as both resource and cautionary tale. Its insight: capital is not a conspiracy of human actors but an impersonal process with its own dynamics. Its error: mistaking description for program.

## Allende's Chile as Counter-Example

Lecture 7 of the course — on the destruction of Chilean democratic socialism — sits alongside the accelerationism lecture as a deliberate contrast. Eden Medina's *Cybernetic Revolutionaries* (also assigned) documents Allende's *Cybersyn* project: a real-time cybernetic management system for the Chilean economy, designed not to serve capital accumulation but to coordinate socialist planning. This is the road not taken: technology accelerated *against* capital rather than *for* it. Its violent termination in 1973 is, for Fisher, the clearest evidence that accelerationism in service of human liberation is not impossible — only that it will be met with force.
