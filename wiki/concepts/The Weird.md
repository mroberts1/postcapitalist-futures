# The Weird

## Definition

The weird is a particular kind of perturbation — a sensation of **wrongness**. A weird entity or object is so strange that it makes us feel it should not exist *here*. Yet if it is here, our conceptual frameworks cannot be valid. The weird thing is not wrong; our conceptions are inadequate.

The weird is distinct from the merely monstrous. Vampires and werewolves are not weird — they have pre-existing lore, protocols for interpretation. A black hole is weirder than a vampire: it belongs to the natural-material cosmos, yet that cosmos must therefore be far stranger than ordinary experience comprehends.

> "The weird is not wrong, after all: it is our conceptions that must be inadequate." — Fisher

---

## Distinction from the Eerie and the Uncanny

The [[The Eerie|eerie]] is constituted by a *failure of agency* — absence where there should be presence, or presence where there should be absence. The weird is constituted by **presence** — the intrusion of the outside into the familiar.

The **uncanny** (*unheimlich*) processes the strange through the inside: strangeness within the familiar, ultimately reduced to castration anxiety or family drama. The weird and eerie move in the opposite direction — they see the inside from the perspective of the outside.

**Weird vs. fantasy:** Fantasy sets its action in wholly separate worlds. The weird requires *egress* — an opening between this world and another. The outside makes an irruption "through time and space, into an objectively familiar locale" (Lévy). This is why Lovecraft set his stories in New England rather than invented lands.

---

## Core Affect: Fascination

The weird cannot only repel — it must also compel. If a story were *merely* horrible, with no element of fascination, it would not be weird.

Fascination in the weird is a form of Lacanian *jouissance*: an enjoyment that entails the inextricability of pleasure and pain. The weird Thing overwhelms, cannot be contained, but fascinates. It sublimates the negative rather than redeeming it: it transforms an object of displeasure into something both terrible and alluring.

The appropriate form for the weird is **montage** — the conjoining of things which do not belong together. Hence surrealism's affinity with the weird.

## Key Figure: H.P. Lovecraft

Lovecraft is Fisher's primary case study for the weird. His distinctive contributions:

- **Hypernaturalism** — not the supernatural but an expanded naturalism; the cosmos is far stranger than ordinary experience grasps
- **Real externality** — "common human laws and interests and emotions have no validity or significance in the vast cosmos-at-large"
- **Egress** — setting stories in New England forces a confrontation between the empirical and the outside
- **The unvisualisable** — Lovecraft's descriptions do not resolve into mental images; the detail exceeds synthesis (cf. Cubism)
- **The Mythos as collective system** — by inter-weaving stories, Lovecraft loses control of his creations to an emerging system that invites collective participation; the Necronomicon seems more real as a cited fragment than it would as a complete text

## Other Key Works and Figures

- **H.G. Wells** — weird against the worldly; the Martian invasion of an ordinary English landscape
- **The Fall** (Mark E. Smith) — grotesque and weird in post-punk; language pushed to the edge of its own wrongness
- **Tim Powers** — time loops, fate, and the weird's relationship to destiny; the archaic meaning of "weird" as fate (cf. the Weird Sisters in Macbeth)
- **Rainer Werner Fassbinder / Philip K. Dick** — simulation and unworlding; the question of what remains when ordinary reality is subtracted
- **David Lynch** (*Mulholland Drive*, *Inland Empire*) — curtains as thresholds; ontological subsidence; the weird as architecture of collapsed hierarchies. In *Inland Empire*, "world-haemorrhaging" becomes so acute there is no stable hierarchy to collapse into.
