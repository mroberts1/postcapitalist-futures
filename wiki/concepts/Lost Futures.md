# Lost Futures

## Definition

Fisher's term for the cultural and temporal condition in which the future has been *cancelled* — not in the sense that time has stopped moving, but in the sense that the cultural expectation of genuine novelty, of a future meaningfully different from the present, has been foreclosed. The phrase "the slow cancellation of the future" comes from Franco 'Bifo' Berardi's *After The Future* (2011), which Fisher adopts and develops.

The lost future is not the past. It is the **future that was expected but never arrived** — the modernist promise of a world transformed by technology and collective organization, the BBC Radiophonic Workshop's sonic vision of a liberated tomorrow, the welfare state's utopia of universal provision. These futures haunt the present not as memories but as absences: the sense that something was promised and not delivered.

---

## The Slow Cancellation

Fisher dates the cancellation to the 1970s-80s: the same period in which [[Capitalist Realism]] was being installed. This is not coincidental. Capitalist realism required the suppression of the expectation that things could be fundamentally different — what [[Acid Communism]] calls "the spectre of a world which could be free."

The cancellation is *slow* because it was not achieved by a single event but through the gradual colonization of cultural possibility: the privatization of the BBC's adventurousness, the defunding of public arts, the market logic applied to education and culture, the replacement of genuine experimentation with product iteration.

The result is what Fisher calls **anachronism and inertia disguised as perpetual change**: constant surface novelty (new products, new formats, new platforms) concealing a deep temporal stasis. The cultural metabolism has slowed.

---

## Popular Music as Index

Fisher uses popular music as the primary evidence for the lost future thesis. The argument: in the 1960s and 70s, music was mutating so rapidly that someone who had been away for a decade would be shocked by what they returned to. The distance between 1956 and 1966 in popular music is enormous; the distance between 2006 and 2016 is comparatively minimal.

This is not simply about quality. It is about the *rate of genuine mutation* — the production of forms that could not have been predicted from what came before. That rate has dramatically slowed. What circulates now is largely recombination of existing forms rather than the emergence of genuinely new ones.

Fisher is careful to distinguish this from the standard complaint that things were better in the past. The point is not evaluative but structural: the cultural conditions that produced genuine novelty — public service broadcasting, art school education, collective musical scenes (the "scenius" rather than the individual genius), the experimental margins of mass media — have been largely destroyed by market logic.

## Hauntology as Cultural Form

[[Hauntology]] is the cultural form of the lost future. Hauntological art does not simply look back with nostalgia; it holds open the wounds of cancelled futures, refusing both the consolations of nostalgia and the false novelty of capitalist cultural churn.

The hauntological works Fisher identifies — Burial's degraded post-rave spectres, The Caretaker's ballroom music consumed by static, Ghost Box Records' phantom BBC modernism — are marked by:
- **Temporal dislocation**: they sound like they come from a time that never quite existed
- **Mourning without resolution**: grief for futures that never arrived, not for pasts that were lost
- **The trace**: a recording quality that emphasizes loss, degradation, incompleteness — the past as partial signal rather than recovered whole

## The Figure of Sapphire and Steel

Fisher's controlling image for the lost future: the final episode of *Sapphire and Steel* (ITV, 1982), in which the two protagonists are trapped forever in a café floating in deep space. Time has stopped. The café is "a pocket, a vacuum" where "there is no time here, not any more." Their situation is described as the trap that is "nowhere, and it's forever."

This is the condition of late capitalist culture: not that time has stopped moving in any literal sense, but that the *future as a direction* — a horizon of genuine possibility — has closed. We live in a perpetual present that resembles a slightly updated version of previous presents. The cultural café of late capitalism is, like Sapphire and Steel's, "still" — frozen at a moment when the future was foreclosed.

Fisher notes: it is harder to believe that *Sapphire and Steel* could have been made now than to believe it was made in 1982. The conditions of public service broadcasting that produced it have gone. This is itself evidence of the lost future thesis.

## Political Dimension

The lost future is not merely a cultural or aesthetic problem. It is the experiential surface of a structural political condition: the elimination of the belief that collective action can produce genuinely different social arrangements.

[[Reflexive impotence]] — the subjective correlate of [[Capitalist Realism]] — is also the subjective correlate of the lost future: the feeling that things cannot be otherwise. Fisher's political wager in his late work ([[Acid Communism]]) is that recovering the sense of *possible futures* — through cultural work as much as through political organizing — is a precondition for any genuinely transformative politics.
