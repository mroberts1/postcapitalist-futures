# Folk Politics

## Definition

The term introduced by Nick Srnicek and Alex Williams in *[[Inventing the Future - Srnicek Williams|Inventing the Future]]* (2015) to name the dominant strategic common sense of the contemporary left — a historically sedimented set of political intuitions that privilege immediacy in all its forms: temporal, spatial, and conceptual. Folk politics is not a single coherent ideology but an *unconscious tendency*, operative across diverse movements.

The core intuition: **immediacy is better and more authentic than abstraction or mediation**. Whatever is local, direct, spontaneous, small-scale, and particular is treated as genuinely political; whatever is large-scale, institutional, abstract, mediated, or universal is treated as suspect or corrupting.

---

## Three Axes

### Temporal Immediacy
- **Reactive** rather than strategic: responding to what corporations and governments initiate rather than setting the agenda
- **Tactical** rather than strategic: single-issue campaigns, process-focus, emphasis on means over ends
- **Ephemeral** forms: occupations, temporary autonomous zones, flash actions
- **Nostalgic**: the return to "good" Keynesian capitalism, the golden age of social democracy, craft production
- **Voluntarist**: romanticisation of riots and insurrection over patient institution-building

### Spatial Immediacy
- **Local** over global: the 100-mile diet, local currencies, community gardens as political acts
- **Small** over large: veneration of small-scale communities and local businesses
- **Unscalable** forms: general assemblies, pure direct democracy, consensus decision-making
- **Exit** over hegemony: withdrawal into autonomous spaces rather than contesting the wider field
- **Direct action**: participants acting themselves, rejecting representation or delegation

### Conceptual Immediacy
- **Everyday** over structural: personal experience over systemic analysis
- **Feeling** over thinking: individual suffering and the affective charge of protest over abstract political economy
- **Particular** over universal: seeing universalism as intrinsically totalitarian
- **Ethical** over political: ethical consumerism, moralising critique of "greedy bankers" rather than structural analysis

---

## Exemplary Manifestations

Srnicek and Williams detect folk-political tendencies (to varying degrees) in:
- **Occupy Wall Street** and Spain's **15M** movement: famous refusal of demands; fetish of horizontalism; occupation as end in itself
- **The Zapatistas**: romantic local autonomy; rejection of scaling up
- **Tiqqun and the Invisible Committee**: left-communist insurrectionism; anti-organisational
- **Alter-globalisation movements**: targeting summits through street confrontations
- **Anarchist-tinged politics**: anti-state, anti-institutional, direct action-centred
- **Localism and slow food**: the authentic particular against the abstraction of global capitalism
- **Ethical consumerism**: individual moral choice as substitute for collective political action

No single position embodies all of these; folk politics is a *tendency* dispersed across positions, not a party line.

---

## Why Folk Politics Arises

Three explanations for its historical emergence:

**1. Response to complexity**: Global capitalism, climate change, and financialisation are genuinely difficult to perceive and analyse — distributed across time and space, involving emergent nonlinear effects that exceed any cognitive map. Folk politics responds by *reducing* complexity to a human scale: targeting a visible local opponent, acting in a tangible immediate space. This feels more real than structural analysis. But the asubjective nature of the system — the fact that "the faces are interchangeable; the power remains the same" — means this reduction falsifies the problem.

**2. Historical reaction to left failures**: Folk politics is partly a response to the disasters of Stalinist state communism (hierarchy → repression) and the exclusions of traditional social democratic parties and trade unions (patriarchal, race-exclusive, Fordist). The tactics of occupations, direct democracy, and anti-hierarchy emerged as *reasonable reactions* to these failures — particularly in the feminist movements and new left of the 1960s–70s. They were appropriate responses to their moment; the problem is their sedimentation into universal strategic wisdom long after the context changed.

**3. Disillusionment with party politics**: Neoliberalism hollowed out electoral alternatives. When social democratic parties converged on the same programme, folk-political immediacy became appealing — at least protest felt *real* compared to the theatre of managed democracy.

---

## The Critique

Srnicek and Williams insist: **folk politics is necessary but insufficient** for any project that aims to transcend capitalism, not merely resist it.

The core problem is *scale*. Capitalism is a genuinely expansionary universal — it permeates and restructures every local space, co-opts every particular resistance, absorbs every cultural alternative. Particularisms can coexist indefinitely with capitalist universalism. Local successes (stopping a hospital closure, blocking a fracking site) are real but do not accumulate into structural transformation: they are overwhelmed by larger dynamics they cannot affect. As Rosa Luxemburg argued, fetishising "immediate results" produces an empty pragmatism that maintains the existing balance of power rather than transforming structural conditions.

Folk-political tactics are also paradoxically aligned with neoliberal ideology in one respect: the argument that global capitalism is too complex to be rationally directed is the standard neoliberal case for markets. Folk politics — retreating to the human-scale and the local — accepts this premise rather than contesting it. The alternative is to *expand humanity's cognitive and political capacities* rather than shrink political ambition to match existing capacities.

The book offers a sharp formulation: folk politics is "politics transmuted into pastime — politics-as-drug-experience" — registered only in the minds of participants, producing affective bonds without structural transformation.

---

## The Alternative

Against folk politics, Srnicek and Williams advocate:
- **Hegemonic strategy**: constructing a counter-hegemonic bloc capable of operating at scale, across coalitions, over decades
- **Institutional work**: think tanks, platforms, educational institutions, media — the patient infrastructure-building that neoliberalism used to build its own hegemony
- **[[Left Modernity]]**: reclaiming the future, universalism, and technological development as left terrain
- **[[Post-Work]]**: a concrete programme of demands (full automation, shorter working week, UBI, cultural shift) as the content of a new hegemonic common sense

The point is not to abandon folk-political tactics (local struggles, direct action, building community) but to *not treat them as sufficient* — to subordinate them to a larger strategic orientation.
