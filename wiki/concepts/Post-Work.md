# Post-Work

## Definition

The political project and social horizon in which wage labour is progressively eliminated as the structuring centre of human life — through automation, reduction of working time, and income decoupled from employment. Post-work is not simply the absence of jobs but a positive vision of social organisation in which free time, care, creative activity, and collective self-determination replace the compulsory drudgery of waged work.

The term and its political programme are most fully elaborated in Srnicek and Williams's *[[Inventing the Future - Srnicek Williams|Inventing the Future]]* (2015), though its intellectual lineage includes Paul Lafargue (*The Right to Be Lazy*, 1883), Keynes's 1930 prediction of 15-hour working weeks by 2030, and Marx's "realm of freedom" beyond the "realm of necessity."

---

## The Post-Work Platform

Srnicek and Williams present four integrated demands — "non-reformist reforms" with a utopian edge grounded in real tendencies:

### 1. [[Full Automation]]
Enthusiastically accelerate automation as a *political demand* rather than fearing it as a threat to jobs. Between 47–80% of current jobs are technically automatable. Full automation is a utopian regulative ideal — reducing necessary labour as much as possible, particularly the most degrading and demeaning forms. The demands form a feedback loop: as wages rise and UBI is implemented, the marginal cost of labour increases, incentivising further automation.

### 2. Shorter Working Week
Return to the historical trajectory of working-time reduction that stalled during the Great Depression (when it had fallen from 60 to 35 hours since 1900). The preferred form is a three-day weekend rather than shorter daily hours (to reduce commuting). Effects: redistributes work in response to automation (preventing unemployment), cuts carbon footprint, improves mental health, and — crucially — builds class power by reducing the supply of labour.

### 3. [[Universal Basic Income]]
An unconditional income sufficient to live on, paid to all, supplementing (not replacing) the welfare state. Transforms precarity from insecurity to voluntary flexibility; partially decommodifies labour; makes class struggle easier (strikes require less strike funds; the disciplinary threat of firing is reduced); recognises unwaged reproductive and care work; feminist in effect (enables exit from dysfunctional relationships, experimentation with family forms).

### 4. Cultural Shift Away from the Work Ethic
The deepest obstacle to post-work is not economic but ideological: work has become the primary site of identity and meaning in capitalist societies; suffering is treated as the precondition of deserving. This must be actively contested through counter-hegemonic cultural work, denaturalising the equation of work with virtue.

The four demands are mutually reinforcing: automation amplifies the case for UBI and shorter hours; shorter hours tighten labour markets; UBI amplifies class power and frees time for politics and care.

## "Non-Reformist Reforms"

The term (from André Gorz) captures the political logic of the post-work platform. These are not system-preserving reforms (social democracy patching capitalism) nor revolutionary demands disconnected from existing tendencies. They are demands that:
1. Have a **utopian edge** that strains at what capitalism can concede — turning polite requests into antagonistic demands
2. Are **grounded in real tendencies** (existing automation, UBI pilots, working-time campaigns)
3. **Shift the equilibrium**: even if not fully achieved, they establish a new political common sense from which further advances become possible

The goal is not communism in five years but a post-*neoliberal* settlement — a "post-work consensus" — from which longer-term transformation can be built.

## Intellectual Lineage

**Marx**: The "realm of freedom" — the sphere of activity beyond necessity, possible only when necessary labour is minimised — is the horizon of communist emancipation. The shortening of the working day is its "basic prerequisite." *Grundrisse*'s "Fragment on Machines" anticipates automation displacing living labour.

**Paul Lafargue** (*The Right to Be Lazy*, 1883): Attacked the "strange delusion" that workers have of a passion for work; demanded a three-hour working day.

**Keynes** (1930): Predicted 15-hour working weeks by 2030 as productivity gains freed humanity from "the economic problem." Widely shared assumption of the time that was then abandoned.

**Autonomist Marxism**: Wages for Housework campaign (Silvia Federici); recognition of unwaged reproductive labour; refusal of work.

**André Gorz**: *Farewell to the Working Class* (1980); non-reformist reforms; the right to income independent of work.

## Connections to Fisher

Mark Fisher's late work converges with the post-work imaginary from a different direction. *[[Acid Communism - Mark Fisher|Acid Communism]]* imagines a "fully automated luxury communism" and a reclaimed consciousness of collective pleasure freed from necessity. Fisher's [[Postcapitalist Desire - Mark Fisher|Postcapitalist Desire]] lectures explicitly discuss automation and the abolition of work. Where Srnicek/Williams offer a strategic-political programme, Fisher offers a cultural and libidinal account of what post-work desire might feel like — the psychedelic socialist imaginary suppressed by neoliberalism.
