# Xenofeminism

## Definition

A feminist political philosophy that embraces technology, rationality, and alienation as tools of emancipation rather than symptoms of oppression. Where some feminist traditions have located liberation in a return to nature, the body, or pre-technological social forms, xenofeminism argues that these are politically regressive: nature is not inherently liberatory, and the appeal to the "natural" has historically been used to enforce gender norms and social hierarchies. The xenofeminist slogan: **"if nature is unjust, change nature."**

Fisher assigned xenofeminism as the final lecture of [[Postcapitalist Desire - Mark Fisher]] — Lecture 15, "Reinventing Prometheus" — framing it as a prospective horizon for the postcapitalist project.

## Laboria Cuboniks and the XF Manifesto

The key text is the **"Xenofeminist Manifesto: A Politics for Alienation"** (2015) by Laboria Cuboniks — a pseudonymous collective of six writers across five countries. The name is an anagram of "Nicolas Bourbaki" (the collective pseudonym used by twentieth-century French mathematicians), signalling the manifesto's commitment to collective, anonymous, rationalist production.

Core arguments:
- **Alienation as resource**: capitalism's alienation of the worker from nature and from "authentic" selfhood is not only a loss but an opportunity — the estrangement from given conditions creates the space to *remake* them
- **Technology as feminist tool**: reproductive technologies, synthetic hormones, digital networks — these are not inherently oppressive but can be appropriated for emancipatory ends; the problem is *who controls them*
- **Anti-naturalism**: "XF seizes alienation as an impetus to generate new universalist politics." The appeal to nature to justify gender or social arrangements is always a political move, not a neutral description
- **Promethean ambition**: the manifesto reclaims the Promethean impulse — stealing fire from the gods, refusing given limits — as a feminist rather than masculinist project

## Lineage: From Firestone to Plant to Cuboniks

Fisher's Lecture 10 (Technofeminism/Cyberfeminism) provides the historical context for xenofeminism:

**Shulamith Firestone** (*The Dialectic of Sex*, 1970): the founding argument for technofeminism — that biological reproduction is the material basis of women's oppression, and that reproductive technology (artificial wombs, etc.) offers a path to genuine liberation. Assigned from the *#ACCELERATE* reader, situating her within the accelerationist lineage.

**Sadie Plant** (*Zeros and Ones*, 1997): cyberfeminist argument that digital technology and the internet are inherently feminizing forces — not because women are more "suited" to them but because networked, non-hierarchical, distributed systems are structurally at odds with patriarchal organization. Plant also connects the history of weaving (Ada Lovelace; the Jacquard loom as the first programmable machine) to the feminization of technical labour.

**Laboria Cuboniks** extends this lineage while refusing both Firestone's biological reductionism and Plant's essentialist cyberfeminism (the idea that digital networks are inherently feminist). XF insists on a *political* rather than technological determinism: technology is a terrain of struggle, not a solution in itself.

## Helen Hester and "Promethean Labours"

Helen Hester's essay "Promethean Labours and Domestic Realism" (also assigned) grounds the xenofeminist project in the politics of domestic and reproductive labour. The "domestic realism" of the title — the cultural insistence that housework and care work are natural, inevitable, and properly feminine — is the ideological target. Against this, Hester argues for a Promethean feminism: one that refuses to accept given conditions and insists on the technological and political transformation of domestic life (automation of household labour, socialization of care, etc.).

This connects directly to Silvia Federici's "Wages Against Housework" (Lecture 6): both are concerned with the political economy of reproductive labour, though Hester/Cuboniks extend Federici's wages-demand toward a more technological horizon.

## Fisher's Interest

Fisher positioned xenofeminism as a live heir to the [[Acid Communism]] project — a contemporary form of the convergence of political consciousness, feminist consciousness-raising, and technological imagination that he saw briefly visible in the late 1960s-70s counterculture. Its anti-naturalism and embrace of alienation as resource resonated with his own anti-nostalgic insistence that the left should not seek to recover a pre-capitalist past but construct genuinely new futures.

The Promethean framing — the title "Reinventing Prometheus" — also echoes [[Accelerationism]]'s inheritance and complicates it: Prometheus as the figure who steals from the gods, who refuses natural limits, but who does so in the service of humanity rather than capital.
