# Hauntology

## Origin

The term was coined by Jacques Derrida in *Spectre of Marx* (1993) as a portmanteau of "haunt" and "ontology" — a mode of being characterised by spectral presences that are neither fully present nor fully absent. The past haunts the present; promises never fulfilled continue to exert pressure; that which was foreclosed returns as a ghost.

## Mark Fisher's Development

Fisher is the key anglophone theorist who gave hauntology its cultural-critical shape, primarily through his blog *k-punk*, *Capitalist Realism* (2009), and *Ghosts of My Life* (2014). For Fisher, hauntology describes the cultural condition under which the future has been cancelled — a sense that we can no longer imagine alternatives to the present order, that we are haunted by the futures that were promised and did not arrive.

The key distinction: hauntology is not nostalgia. Nostalgia mourns a past that once existed. Hauntology is haunted by a **future that never arrived** — the BBC Radiophonic Workshop's modernist promise, the welfare-state utopia, the sense that technology and collective organization would produce genuine liberation. These are not memories but *absences*: the ghost of what should have been.

Fisher associated hauntology especially with a strand of British music — Ghost Box Records, Burial, The Caretaker — characterised by:
- Degraded, spectral recordings of previous cultural moments
- A sense of [[Lost Futures]] (the BBC Radiophonic Workshop; the modernist promise of the post-war welfare state)
- Memory as distortion, as incomplete trace, rather than faithful retrieval

### Key Musical Figures (from *Ghosts of My Life*)
- **Burial** — sonic ghost of rave's collective euphoria, dispersed into post-urban loneliness; vinyl crackle as hauntological signature
- **The Caretaker** — ballroom music consumed by static; memory as active forgetting
- **Ghost Box Records (Belbury Poly, The Focus Group)** — phantom BBC modernism; nostalgia for a modernist future that never arrived
- **John Foxx** — precursor figure; the first to get there without the context to be understood
- **Tricky** — haunted by his dead mother's voice; *Maxinquaye* as purgatorial hauntological landscape

## Relationship to the Eerie

Hauntology and [[The Eerie]] are adjacent but distinct:

- Both are concerned with presences and absences that trouble the present
- The eerie is the more general aesthetic concept (defined by the failure of presence/absence and the problem of agency)
- Hauntology is specifically about **cultural and historical** haunting — the way foreclosed futures return, the way the past persists as trace, the way institutions carry the ghosts of abandoned promises

An eerie landscape is not necessarily hauntological. But the hauntological is almost always eerie — the ghost of a future that never arrived, the BBC Radiophonic sound that should be from the past but feels like a lost future, are failures of presence in Fisher's sense.

Capital is both an eerie entity (for Fisher in *The Weird and the Eerie*) and a hauntological force (in *Capitalist Realism*): conjured from nothing, it haunts the present by foreclosing alternatives.

## Relationship to Capitalist Realism

Hauntology and [[Capitalist Realism]] are two faces of the same condition. Capitalist realism forecloses the future politically (making alternatives unthinkable); hauntology is the cultural experience of that foreclosure. The ghost that haunts is not some pre-capitalist past but the future-oriented promise of modernity itself — the sense that things were supposed to get genuinely better.

Fisher's concept of [[Lost Futures]] bridges the two: the slow cancellation of the future (diagnosed politically in *Capitalist Realism*) is experienced culturally as hauntology — the sense of living amid the ruins of futures that never arrived.
