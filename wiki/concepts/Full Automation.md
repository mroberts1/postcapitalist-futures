# Full Automation

## Definition

The political demand to use technological development to progressively eliminate human labour from production, distribution, and services — with the aim of freeing humanity from drudgery rather than generating unemployment and precarity. Full automation is the first and foundational demand of the [[Post-Work]] platform as articulated by Srnicek and Williams in *[[Inventing the Future - Srnicek Williams|Inventing the Future]]*.

The key political move: treating automation as a *left political demand* rather than a capitalist threat to jobs. Where the standard left response to automation is defensive (protect existing jobs, retrain workers, slow the pace of change), Srnicek and Williams argue this is strategically catastrophic — automation cannot be stopped, and defending jobs for their own sake means defending drudgery.

---

## Historical Trajectory

Capitalism has always been driven by the imperative to replace living labour with capital:
- **19th century**: Agricultural mechanisation; breakdown of craft into deskilled tasks; factory machinery
- **Early 20th century**: Automation of routine manual tasks (hauling, conveying); expansion of office work and skilled labour
- **Mid 20th century**: Microprocessors begin replacing semiskilled service workers (telephone operators, secretaries)
- **Late 20th–early 21st century**: Roboticisation of manufacturing reaches saturation; service robots proliferate; routine cognitive work (legal research, financial analysis, journalism) begins to be automated

**Current wave**: Machine learning, deep learning, advanced robotics, and big data coalesce into a "second machine age" that can automate:
- Non-routine manual tasks (self-driving vehicles)
- Non-routine cognitive tasks (pattern recognition, legal research, news writing)
- Management and decision-making (algorithmic trading, AI customer service)
- Distribution (logistics revolution, automated warehouses)

Result: Not just polarisation of the labour market (mid-skill jobs eliminated, leaving high-skill and low-skill) but potential decimation of the low-skill end as well. Between 47–80% of current jobs technically automatable by existing or near-term technology.

## Why Automation Has Stalled

Despite technological potential, full automation has not arrived. Reasons:
1. **Low wages suppress investment**: When labour is cheap, there is no incentive to invest in capital. The logical complement of demanding full automation is demanding *higher wages* — making labour expensive enough to incentivise replacement.
2. **Productivity lag**: New technologies take 5–15 years to be integrated into organisational processes. The current AI wave is too new to appear in productivity figures.
3. **Political choice**: Of US firms that could benefit from industrial robots, fewer than 10% had adopted them. Automation requires political direction, not just market incentives.

---

## Limits and Complications

**Technical limits**: Machines remain worse than humans at tacit knowledge, genuine creativity, highly flexible physical work, and most affective labour. Full automation is a utopian regulative ideal, not an achievable endpoint in the near term.

**Economic limits**: Some tasks can already be automated but aren't because human labour is cheaper. Addressed by raising wages and subsidising automation.

**Ethical limits — care work**: The strongest objection is that some labour (raising children, care for the elderly and disabled) should be performed by humans for ethical reasons. Two responses:
1. In a post-work society, care labour could be given *greater* social value — free time created by automation could enable more, not less, human care
2. More radically: the equation of "natural" care with female bodies is ideologically loaded; automation of domestic labour could be a feminist project, as could rethinking reproductive technology (connections to [[Xenofeminism]])

## "Fully Automated Luxury Communism"

The slogan *Fully Automated Luxury Communism* (FALC) — popularised by Aaron Bastani around 2015 — names the utopian endpoint toward which full automation points: a world where machines produce abundance and humans are freed for leisure, culture, and care, with social goods distributed equitably rather than through the market. FALC combines the left-accelerationist embrace of technology with communist political economy.

Fisher's [[Acid Communism]] imagines a related horizon from a different direction: not the technological programme but the *psychic and cultural* conditions of freedom from necessity.
