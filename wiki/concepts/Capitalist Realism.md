# Capitalist Realism

## Definition

The condition in which capitalism presents itself not as one political-economic option among others but as the *ground of reality itself* — coextensive with common sense, practicality, and what is possible. As Fisher formulates it: **it is easier to imagine the end of the world than the end of capitalism**.

Capitalist realism is not a propaganda system requiring active belief. It operates as an *atmosphere* — a pervasive naturalization of capitalist assumptions that works even through cynicism, irony, and explicit anti-capitalism. One does not need to believe in capitalism for capitalist realism to function; one only needs to act within its frameworks while maintaining an ironic distance. This is precisely what Žižek means when he says that ideology is not "they don't know what they are doing, but they are doing it anyway" but rather "they know very well what they're doing, and they're doing it anyway."

---

## Origins of the Term

The term was used in the 1960s by German Pop artists and by Michael Schudson (1984) to make parodic reference to socialist realism. Fisher's use is different: he gives it an "exorbitant" meaning — not confined to art or advertising but describing a total ideological condition.

## Key Features

### Business Ontology
The assumption — now so naturalized it is no longer experienced as an assumption — that everything in society should be run as a business: healthcare, education, public broadcasting, welfare. This is not argued for but *presupposed*, operative even when no one explicitly makes the case.

### The Suppression of Alternatives
Capitalist realism does not simply prefer capitalism to alternatives; it makes alternatives *literally unthinkable*. This is achieved not through censorship but through the slow colonization of the imagination: the very frameworks available for thinking about social organization are capitalist frameworks.

### Interpassivity
Cultural products (films, art, NGO campaigns) that perform critique of capitalism on the audience's behalf, allowing continued participation with impunity. *Wall-E*'s anti-consumerist satire, Bono's Product Red — these reinforce rather than challenge capitalist realism by providing outlets for critical affect that cost nothing and change nothing.

### The Privatization of Stress
Social and structural problems — mental illness, addiction, educational failure — are systematically reframed as individual failures of neurochemistry or personal responsibility. This forecloses political analysis: if depression is a chemical imbalance, the question of *why so many people are depressed under this system* cannot be posed.

## Capitalism and the Real (Lacanian Dimension)

Fisher uses the Lacanian distinction between the **Real** and **reality**:
- *Reality* is the constructed, symbolically mediated social world
- *The Real* is what any given reality must suppress in order to constitute itself

Capitalist realism *is* capitalist reality — the symbolic construction through which capitalism presents itself as natural. Its **Real** (what it must suppress) includes:
1. The mental health epidemic — evidence that the system is dysfunctional rather than optimal
2. Environmental catastrophe — evidence that the system's growth imperative is unsustainable
3. The persistence and proliferation of bureaucracy — evidence that the "frictionless market" never arrived

These aporias are not accidental failures but structural features. Showing them to be contradictions internal to capitalism — rather than accidents or individual failures — is the primary task of anti-capitalist politics under conditions of capitalist realism.

## Bureaucracy and Market Stalinism

Neoliberalism promised to abolish bureaucracy. Instead, bureaucracy has proliferated in new, decentralized forms: targets, audits, self-assessments, "aims and objectives", performance reviews with hidden expectations (the "seven pieces of flair" problem). Fisher calls this **Market Stalinism**: the Stalinist fetish for symbolic achievement over real achievement, transplanted into a nominally anti-Stalinist framework.

The key mechanism: workers become their own auditors — the surveillance apparatus is *internalized*. This corresponds to Foucault's account of the panopticon: power operates most efficiently when subjects cannot tell whether they are being observed, and so must act as if they always are.

## Reflexive Impotence

The subjective correlate of capitalist realism. Those who live under it — especially the young — are not apathetic but **reflexively impotent**: they know things are bad, and they know (or believe) that nothing can be done. This belief is self-fulfilling. Combined with **depressive hedonia** — the inability to do anything other than pursue low-intensity pleasure — it produces a population of disaffected consumers who cannot locate or sustain political agency.

---

## Challenges to Capitalist Realism

Capitalist realism cannot be overcome by moral critique (which it absorbs: poverty, famine and war become part of the accepted texture of reality). It can only be threatened by demonstrating its *internal inconsistencies* — showing that its "realism" is itself ideologically constructed, that what it presents as necessary is contingent, and that what it presents as impossible is in fact achievable.

Fisher's own candidates for productive aporias: the mental health epidemic and the bureaucratic proliferation. Both are widely experienced; neither has yet been sufficiently politicized.

The 2008 credit crisis represented, for Fisher, a moment when capitalist realism's ideological cover collapsed — though without a credible alternative, it survived as an "undead default", continuing to govern political-economic life without conviction or forward momentum.
