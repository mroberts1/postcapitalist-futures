# The Eerie

## Definition

The eerie is an aesthetic mode constituted by a **failure of absence** or a **failure of presence**:

- **Failure of absence:** Something is present where there should be nothing. A sense that there is more in (or behind) a thing than mere mechanism — some form of intent or agency we cannot quite identify.
- **Failure of presence:** Nothing is present where there should be something. A gap where an agent should be, or once was.

Both modes turn on the central enigma of **agency**: *Is there an agent here at all? What kind? What does it want?* The eerie necessarily involves speculation and suspense — once the questions are resolved and agency is identified, the eerie immediately dissipates.

Not all mysteries are eerie. There must also be a sense of **alterity** — a feeling that the enigma involves forms of knowledge, subjectivity, or sensation that lie beyond common experience.

> "The eerie concerns the most fundamental metaphysical questions one could pose, questions to do with existence and non-existence: Why is there something here when there should be nothing? Why is there nothing here when there should be something?" — Fisher

---

## Distinction from the Weird and the Uncanny

The [[The Weird|weird]] is constituted by *presence* — the intrusion of that which does not belong. The eerie is constituted by a *problem of agency*, which may manifest as uncanny presence or as uncanny absence.

The **uncanny** (*unheimlich*, Freud) makes the strange familiar again, folding the outside back into domestic and psychological explanation. The eerie does the opposite: it looks at the inside from the perspective of the outside, and leaves the question of agency open.

The eerie seldom clings to enclosed domestic spaces. It is found more readily in **landscapes partially emptied of the human** — coastal terrain, moorland, ruins, stone circles, archaeological sites.

---

## Eerie Calm and Its Affect

The eerie is not primarily terrifying. Its characteristic affect is closer to **apprehension mixed with allure** — "eerie calm" names a state of detachment from mundane urgencies. This detachment is the precondition for an openness to forces ordinarily concealed.

The eerie can give us access to the forces that govern mundane reality from below or beyond — which is why Fisher connects it to the critique of capitalism. It can also be a **liberatory** experience: Joan Lindsay's *Picnic at Hanging Rock* presents an eerie outside that is as much a release from the petty confines of ordinary life as it is a danger.

## The Two Modes in Detail

### Failure of Absence: Something Where There Should Be Nothing

An eerie cry — the paradigm case. A bird's call is eerie when it suggests deliberative intent where only mechanism should be. The enigma: is there an agent behind this? What kind?

**Key examples:**
- Daphne du Maurier's "The Birds" — birds develop collective agency across species; the BBC's progressive silence removes all explanatory authority; the story ends without resolution
- The eerie cry or sound in landscape (cf. Eno's *On Land*, the track "Shadow")
- The feeling attached to stone circles: not "why did the builders disappear?" but "what kind of being made this?"
- Capital itself: an entity conjured from nothing that exerts more influence than any substantial thing

### Failure of Presence: Nothing Where There Should Be Something

Ruins, abandoned structures, the *Marie Celeste*. The mystery of the *Marie Celeste* — never resolved — saturates the ship with the eerie. Stone circles pose a different version: we are not asking what happened to the builders but what *kind* of being they were. Their symbolic order has rotted away; what remains is unintelligible and inscrutable.

**Key examples:**
- Stonehenge, Easter Island: traces of a departed agent whose purposes are unknown
- Christopher Priest's *The Glamour*: glamour (invisibility) as negative hallucination — not seeing what is there
- Daphne du Maurier's "Don't Look Now": fate as eerie agent, weaving patterns only visible too late
- Margaret Atwood's *Surfacing*: the narrator as an eerie gap in the dominant structure, unable to feel what she is supposed to feel
- Abandoned ship *Marie Celeste*: the irreducible, unresolved mystery

## The Eerie and Agency

The question of agency is the engine of the eerie at every level:

1. **Is there an agent at all?** (failure of absence mode)
2. **What kind of agent?** (failure of presence mode — we know something acted, but its nature is opaque)
3. **Who or what is the agent operating within us?** (the unconscious, the death drive, fate)

Fisher connects this to Freud's Thanatos and to Radical Enlightenment questions: to what extent can intentionality be attributed to anything — humans included?

**Eerie Thanatos** — the concept developed through Nigel Kneale and Alan Garner: a transpersonal, transtemporal death drive operating through inorganic artefacts and landscape. In *The Stone Tape*, matter records traumatic events; the scientists are drawn into a compulsion to repeat them. In Garner's *Red Shift*, mythic patterns abduct individuals out of linear time and into a fatal simultaneity.

## The Eerie and Landscape

The eerie attaches especially to **partially emptied landscapes** — spaces at the edge of the human. The Suffolk coast (M.R. James, Brian Eno's *On Land*), the Canadian wilderness (*Surfacing*), the Scottish Highlands (*Under the Skin*), the Estonian industrial wasteland transformed into the Zone (*Stalker*).

Fisher argues that landscape functions as an agent in these contexts, not a backdrop. Eno's manifesto for *On Land*: "the landscape has ceased to be a backdrop for something else to happen in front of; instead, everything that happens is a part of the landscape."

Particular terrains are **stained by traumatic events** — stone circles as scar tissue recording prehistoric catastrophes (Kneale's final *Quatermass*); Sutton Hoo as burial mound; Dunwich as a city largely consumed by the sea, its absence more palpable than presence.

---

## The Eerie and Inorganic Agency

A recurring motif: **inorganic artefacts** that carry ancient agency and draw the living into their patterns:

- M.R. James: bronze whistle, ancient crown — unearthing these objects calls up forces
- Nigel Kneale (*The Stone Tape*): haunted room as recording medium; stone as tape; the human nervous system as player — which the recording eventually plays
- Alan Garner (*Red Shift*): Neolithic votive axe operating across three historical periods simultaneously; the axe as trigger for mythic repetition
- Reza Negarestani's term: **inorganic demons** — autonomous, sentient relics that generate effects out of the human host

## The Eerie and Memory / Amnesia

Christopher Priest's novels explore the eerie of *gaps in identity*:

- **Confabulation** — the process by which stories are constructed to fill memory gaps, without any unified self as author
- **Negative hallucination** — not seeing what is there; Freud's concept; a gap that conceals itself
- **The Glamour**: glamour (an old Scottish word for an enchantment making someone invisible) as a failure of presence that cannot be perceived

The eerie of amnesia: identity is maintained by stories, and when those stories fail, the question of agency becomes unavoidable — *who* is producing these confabulations? There is not so much an author as a confabulatory process without any "one" behind it.

## The Eerie in Film

### Kubrick
- *2001: A Space Odyssey* — the monolith as paradigm eerie object: its rectilinearity and gloss force inference of a higher intelligence, whose nature and purpose remain entirely undisclosed; Ligeti's micropolyphony reinforces the sense of alterity
- *The Shining* — the Overlook Hotel as recording system (cf. *The Stone Tape*); forces of unknown nature and intent; Jack's question "who's buying?" is left permanently unresolved; aeonic time vs. linear time; the fateful question of how far the hotel's influence extends back

### Tarkovsky
- *Solaris* — the sentient ocean as alien intelligence operating through human memories; the simulacrum of Hari (missing zip on her dress — constructed from incomplete memory); the impasse of mismatching intelligences
- *Stalker* — the Zone as eerie space without special effects; Tarkovsky used an atmospheric Estonian location; the stalker as ethics of the eerie — humble, alert, dedicated to exploring the outside; the Room that grants deepest wishes as a judgement on being

### Glazer
- *Under the Skin* — alien-among-us; information restriction as eerie technique; the Johansson character as analogue of the camera; naturalism becomes denaturalizing; the black ooze capture sequences as eerie ellipses; the body as prosthesis

### Nolan
- *Interstellar* — love as eerie agent; Miller's Planet as degree-zero of agency; the Tesseract (time as space); "They" as future humans whose nature is never disclosed

## The Eerie's Positive Valence

Fisher argues explicitly against the assumption that the eerie is always threatening or malign:

- **Eerie calm** — a serenity of detachment, not of comfort
- **Joan Lindsay (*Picnic at Hanging Rock*)** — the eerie outside has a "languorous and delirious allure"; the disappearance is not presented as punishment but as a passage beyond the "petty repressions and mean confines of common experience"
- **Atwood (*Surfacing*)** — the narrator's affective dyslexia as a trace of "an as yet unimaginable outside" rather than a pathology requiring cure
- **Tarkovsky's Stalker** — the Zone as a space of faith and transformation as well as danger; the stalker's relationship to it as openness, not mastery
