# Hyperstition

## Definition

A **hyperstition** is a fiction that works to make itself true — a belief or narrative that, by being widely held and acted upon, catalyses the conditions for its own realisation. The term was coined in the late 1990s by the **Cybernetic Culture Research Unit (CCRU)** at Warwick, the experimental theory collective that included Nick Land, Sadie Plant, Mark Fisher, and others.

The temporal logic of hyperstition is the "will have been": it acts in the present as if the future it projects already exists, creating the social and material conditions through which the future retroactively validates the fiction. It differs from both straightforward lying (which aims to deceive) and utopian fantasy (which remains purely imaginary) by functioning as an *operative* fiction.

---

## CCRU Origins

At the CCRU, hyperstition was theorised in the context of accelerationist thought and Landian capitalism-as-horror: capitalism itself operates hyperstitionally, as an alien intelligence that recruits human agents by feeding them fictions (the future profit, the approaching singularity) that then motivate the behaviours that make the fictions real. The Terminator film franchise was a canonical example: time-travel paradoxes in which the future sends back agents that create the conditions for the future that sent them.

Hyperstition in this original sense had a horror-inflected, inhuman valence — the fiction takes on a life of its own, exceeding the intentions of any individual agent.

## Srnicek and Williams: Hyperstitional Progress

In *[[Inventing the Future - Srnicek Williams|Inventing the Future]]*, Srnicek and Williams appropriate the concept for a left political project, stripping it of the Land-inflected nihilism and using it to theorise how the left can construct *visions of the future* that become materially effective.

The key move: progress is not a pre-given property of history (a necessary teleological arc) but neither is it mere fantasy. Progress is **hyperstitional** — it must be *constructed* through political action, but enticing fictions of a better future are an indispensable part of the construction. They work by:
- Orienting dispersed political energies in a common direction
- Making the future an "active historical force in the present"
- Creating criteria for adjudicating which struggles to support
- Generating popular consent for the sacrifices required by long-term political work

The [[Post-Work]] platform is explicitly hyperstitional: full automation, UBI, and the shorter working week are not inevitable outcomes of technological development but political demands that must be made real through collective action — yet articulating them as a coherent future helps constitute the political forces that might achieve them.

---

## Distinction from Mere Propaganda

Hyperstitions are not simply propaganda or lies. Three features distinguish them:

1. **Grounded in real tendencies**: The post-work hyperstition is grounded in existing automation trends, UBI pilots, and working-time research — it is not fantasy but extrapolation.
2. **Self-aware**: A hyperstition does not require its agents to believe it is already true; it requires only that they act *as if* building toward the future it projects.
3. **Performative**: It operates through collective enactment, not individual belief — the more people act on it, the more real the conditions it projects become.

---

## Relation to Fisher

Mark Fisher at the CCRU was involved in the original theorisation of hyperstition. His [[Lost Futures]] analysis could be read as a diagnosis of a *failed* hyperstition: the future-oriented fictions of the postwar left (social democracy, communism, counterculture) were operative hyperstitions that built real social forces — until neoliberalism's counter-hyperstitions displaced them, and [[Hauntology]] became the culture of living in their aftermath.

[[Acid Communism]] can be read as Fisher's attempt at a new left hyperstition: the "spectre of a world which could be free," made present enough in cultural memory to become politically operative again.
