# Acid Communism

## Definition

Fisher's name for a political concept-in-formation at the time of his death: **the spectre of a world which could be free**. The term is deliberately provocative — "a joke of sorts, but one with very serious purpose." It names the convergence, once partially visible in the 1960s-70s and since suppressed, of:

1. **Class consciousness** — organized political demand for economic equality
2. **Socialist-feminist consciousness-raising** — politicization of personal experience and subjectivity
3. **Psychedelic consciousness** — dissolution of ego-boundaries, transformation of perception, opening to collective experience

Acid communism is both a description of historical tendencies and a *virtual* concept: a potential that was never fully actualized but that continues to exert pressure on the present. The aim is not to repeat the 1960s counterculture but to **unforget** the possibilities it briefly materialized — to counter-exorcise the spectre that neoliberalism has been working to eliminate.

---

## The Exorcism

The central claim of Fisher's unfinished book: the last forty years have been a project of exorcising "the spectre of a world which could be free" (Marcuse, *Eros and Civilisation*). Neoliberalism's real target was not Soviet communism or Keynesian social democracy — both were already weakening under their own contradictions — but the experiments in democratic socialism and libertarian communism visible in the late 1960s and early 1970s.

The **Pinochet coup (1973)** is the founding event of this exorcism in its most violent form: the military destruction of Allende's democratic socialist government in Chile, with direct US support. The message was unambiguous — an actually existing alternative to capitalism would be terminated. In the US and UK, the installation of capitalist realism was more gradual, combining inducements (consumer culture, aspirational individualism) with repression (union-breaking, public sector defunding). The ultimate effect was the same: the extirpation of even the *idea* that another arrangement was possible.

## Marcuse as Resource

Fisher's central theoretical resource is Herbert Marcuse, whose *Eros and Civilisation* he treats as the key text for recovering what was at stake:

- **Surplus repression**: capitalism requires more repression of human drives than civilization actually needs — specifically, a scarcity of *time*, keeping people in unnecessary labour to prevent them discovering the immanent possibility of freedom. "Capitalism: a system that generates artificial scarcity in order to produce real scarcity."

- **The Great Refusal**: art constitutes a rational negation of the existing order — not as explicit propaganda but as a formal incompatibility with the reductive administration of life under capital. Experimental art, Fisher notes following Adorno and Marcuse, is not politically effective because it *argues* for alternatives but because it *embodies* them.

- **The neutralization of the aesthetic challenge**: Marcuse already anticipated the counterculture's failure. The absorption of oppositional culture into commercial space converts images of "another way of life" into "freaks or types of the same life." The gangster, the beatnik, the vamp — and later, the punk, the raver, the freaker.

## The 1960s as Incomplete Project

Fisher insists the 1960s should be understood not as a cautionary tale (counterculture → neoliberalism) but as an incomplete project. The potentials materialized then — mass access to higher education, leisure time, new collective social forms, a genuine expansion of what it meant to be alive — had to be continuously suppressed because they pointed beyond capital.

The Danny Baker passage (from Baker's memoir): July 1966, a working-class family on a canal holiday, a transistor radio playing "River Deep Mountain High" then "Bus Stop". What made the moment possible: existential security (full employment, cheap housing, welfare state), genuinely new music, a sense of "exorbitant sufficiency" — the present as complete in itself, open rather than foreclosed.

Fisher's argument: such moments were not accidents but achievements of specific political conditions that neoliberalism has systematically dismantled — and then declared impossible in principle.

---

## The 1970s and the Counter-Revolution

Fisher is especially insistent that the 1970s needs to be reclaimed from reactionary narratives. Recent historical work (Andy Beckett's *When the Lights Went Out*, Jefferson Cowie's *Stayin' Alive*, John Medhurst's *That Option No Longer Exists*) shows a decade of genuine struggle, in which the meaning and legacy of the 1960s was actively contested. The Miners' Strike of 1972 saw alliances between striking miners and students that echoed Paris 1968.

Neoliberalism was not a natural economic correction but a **political counter-revolution**, prosecuted with what Fisher calls "bravura intelligence, ferocious energy and improvisational imagination." The think-tank as Leninist vanguard. The cultural battle for hearts and minds fought through tabloids, television, and the long-term defunding of public culture.

---

## Consciousness-Raising as Political Method

One of acid communism's three components is specifically methodological: **consciousness-raising**, developed in the socialist-feminist movement of the late 1960s-70s. The practice of transforming personal experience into political analysis — recognizing that private suffering has structural causes — is precisely what [[Capitalist Realism]] prevents through the [[privatization of stress]].

Fisher saw consciousness-raising as a model for how class consciousness and mental health politics might converge: understanding depression, anxiety, and reflexive impotence not as individual neurochemical failures but as the accumulated psychic cost of living under capital.

---

## Relationship to Other Fisher Concepts

Acid communism is the constructive answer to a question *Capitalist Realism* poses without fully answering: what comes after? Where hauntology mourns cancelled futures and capitalist realism diagnoses the present, acid communism names the latent possibilities that the present suppresses — and proposes their recovery as political project.

| Concept | Orientation | Function |
|---|---|---|
| Capitalist Realism | Diagnostic | Names the condition |
| Lost Futures | Elegiac | Mourns what was cancelled |
| Acid Communism | Constructive | Recovers what can be unforgotten |

## Blog and publication trajectory

A timeline of the concept's development across Fisher's k-punk writing and published work. The concept was never named on the blog — the explicit framing only arrives in the 2016 manuscript — but the core intuitions emerge as early as 2005 and develop steadily through the political upheavals of 2015.

---

### 2005 — Left Hyperstition: the method before the concept

[[k-punk/raw/posts/2005-11-28-left-hyperstition-1-the-fictions-of-capital|Left Hyperstition 1: The Fictions of Capital]] · [[k-punk/raw/posts/2005-11-29-left-hyperstition-2-be-unrealistic-change-whats-possible|Left Hyperstition 2: Be Unrealistic, Change What's Possible]]

Fisher is working on the *political epistemology* that acid communism will later presuppose. Capital operates hyperstically — through behavioral compliance rather than conscious belief. People "know perfectly well" money is a token yet act as if it has substance. The implication: liberation requires practicing new behaviors, not just arguing for them. Counter-capitalism needs to install new sublimations, not only critique existing ones. The slogan is an inversion of the Situationist formula: not "be realistic, demand the impossible" but *be unrealistic, make the possible real*. This is already the method of acid communism — unforgetting as practice — without yet having the historical content (Marcuse, the 1960s) that would give it body.

---

### 2006 — We want it all: the working-class will-to-greatness

[[k-punk/raw/posts/2006-02-12-we-want-it-all|We want it all]]

The earliest post tagged #acid-communism, and the clearest pre-conceptual statement of the project. Prompted by Celebrity Big Brother and the closure of *Smash Hits*, Fisher locates in the history of working-class popular culture a drive that capitalism has now suppressed: the proletarian aspiration to *be more* — to negate Social Facts through Sonic Fictions, to demand the excellent rather than the merely representative. Against Nietzsche's claim that egalitarianism necessarily levels down, Fisher argues that popular culture once demonstrated the opposite: a "paradoxically egalitarian aristocracy" that was genuinely of the working class and genuinely ambitious for transformation. Big Brother's Celebreality is the index of defeat — it has substituted the mirror of ordinary for the screen of the extraordinary. The lost Pop imaginary is already the "spectre of a world which could be free."

---

### 2009 — If I was a communist: engaging the idea directly

[[k-punk/raw/posts/2009-03-18-if-i-was-a-communist|If I was a communist…]]

Brief but significant: Fisher reports on the *Idea of Communism* conference at Birkbeck (Badiou, Žižek, Toscano, Bosteels). His Frieze piece on the conference is the closest he comes on the blog to directly engaging communist possibility as political horizon rather than as object of cultural analysis. The promised follow-up — "Eliminativists Like Us" — was never published, but the engagement marks a turning point: Fisher is now moving from diagnosing capitalist realism to asking what thinking beyond it would require.

---

### 2015 — Democracy is Joy: Spinoza and collective emotion

[[k-punk/raw/posts/2015-07-13-democracy-is-joy|Democracy is Joy]]

The post closest to the acid communism project in all but name, written in the context of the Greek referendum and the No vote. Fisher makes the move that will define the book: from political economy to *affect*. Neoliberalism since 2010 has abandoned seduction — it operates purely through threat. The No vote was significant not because it immediately won anything but because it demonstrated that "alternative emotions are contagious" — that collective self-determination produces a joy that the system cannot absorb. Spinoza is introduced as the key philosophical resource: emotions emerge from analyzable causal fields and can be collectively engineered toward liberation rather than captured by capital. The essay closes with a direct invocation of communist possibility ("technological development could liberate humanity for meaningful work under collective ownership") — the argument of the book in compressed form.

---

### 2016 — Acid Communism (introduction): the full theoretical framework

[[Acid Communism - Mark Fisher]]

The unfinished book's introduction, circulated before Fisher's death and published in *K-Punk: The Collected and Unpublished Writings* (2018). The Left Hyperstition method, the working-class Pop imaginary from "We want it all," and the Spinozist affect theory of "Democracy is Joy" are now explicitly organized around Marcuse's *Eros and Civilization*, consciousness-raising as political method, and a materialist reading of the 1960s and 70s as a suppressed political tendency rather than a cautionary cultural tale. The concept is named and theorized for the first time.

---

### 2016–17 — Postcapitalist Desire: the course as intellectual testament

[[Postcapitalist Desire - Mark Fisher]]

Fisher's final Goldsmiths course, the closest thing to a complete map of the acid communism project. Lectures 2–8 trace the argument of the introduction; Lectures 9–15 extend it into post-Fordism, technofeminism, accelerationism, and xenofeminism. The course was interrupted by Fisher's death in January 2017 four sessions in; students and collaborators continued it as "Nothings Into Somethings." Published posthumously (Repeater Books, 2020), it represents the fullest elaboration of what Fisher was moving toward: not a nostalgia for the 1960s but a recovery of its latent possibilities — "unforgetting" as the political project that his whole trajectory had been building toward.
