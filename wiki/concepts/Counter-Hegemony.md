# Counter-Hegemony

## Definition

The project of constructing a rival political common sense capable of displacing an existing hegemonic order. Drawn from Gramsci's concept of **hegemony** — the way a ruling class maintains dominance not just through force but through the active consent of the governed, by shaping what counts as common sense, natural, and realistic — counter-hegemony names the strategic ambition to build an alternative common sense capable of winning popular consent and eventually displacing the existing order.

In the context of *[[Inventing the Future - Srnicek Williams|Inventing the Future]]*, counter-hegemony is the strategic mode Srnicek and Williams advocate as the alternative to [[Folk Politics]].

---

## Gramsci and Hegemony

Antonio Gramsci (1891–1937), writing in fascist prison, developed the concept to explain why socialist revolution had not followed the Russian model in Western Europe despite capitalist crisis. The answer: capitalist hegemony. In advanced capitalist societies, the ruling class governs through *civil society* (churches, schools, media, cultural institutions) as well as the state — shaping the common sense in which political life takes place. Workers don't need to be coerced into accepting capitalism; they actively consent to it through the frameworks available for understanding the world.

Counter-hegemonic strategy therefore cannot focus only on seizing state power ("war of manoeuvre") but must involve a long "war of position" — contesting terrain across civil society, building new institutions, creating alternative common senses that gradually delegitimise the existing order.

---

## Laclau and Mouffe

Ernesto Laclau and Chantal Mouffe (*Hegemony and Socialist Strategy*, 1985) updated Gramsci for a post-Marxist framework: there is no privileged subject of revolution (the working class), no teleological guarantee. Hegemony works through the construction of **equivalential chains** — linking diverse demands and identities into a provisional "people" that defines itself against a common antagonist. The universal (the hegemonic project) is always occupied by a particular (a specific coalition and programme) through struggle.

This is the theoretical background for Srnicek/Williams's emphasis on building a *populist* left project — one that articulates diverse demands (post-work, feminism, environmentalism, anti-racism) into a coherent platform capable of generating mass consent.

---

## Neoliberalism as Model

A central argument of *Inventing the Future* is that the left should study *how neoliberalism built its hegemony* as a model for counter-hegemonic strategy — not to copy its content but to learn its methods:

- **Decades-long horizon**: The Mont Pèlerin Society (founded 1947) spent 30 years building intellectual infrastructure before Thatcher and Reagan implemented the programme
- **Think tanks and universities**: IEA, Heritage Foundation, Adam Smith Institute — manufacturing and disseminating an alternative common sense
- **Patience with temporary marginalisation**: Operating as a minority tendency while the postwar Keynesian consensus held, waiting for a crisis moment when neoliberal ideas could fill the vacuum
- **Crisis opportunism**: The stagflation of the 1970s created the opening; neoliberal ideas were ready to occupy it

The left's failure in the same period: it retained folk-political intuitions, rejected institutional infrastructure as corrupting, and had nothing ready when the Keynesian order collapsed.

---

## Strategic Components

Srnicek and Williams sketch two complementary elements:

**Plumbing**: Durable infrastructure — think tanks, media outlets, educational institutions, platforms, unions, parties. This is the slow work that creates the conditions for hegemonic shift. Folk politics systematically undervalues this, preferring the spectacular confrontation to the unglamorous work of institution-building.

**Ignition**: Moments of mass mobilisation that build coalitions, energise a constituency, and shift the political weather. These are not self-sufficient (folk politics' error is treating them as ends in themselves) but are essential for converting institutional work into mass political force.

The goal: a **post-work consensus** analogous to the postwar Keynesian consensus and the neoliberal consensus — a new hegemonic common sense that transforms the parameters within which politics operates.
