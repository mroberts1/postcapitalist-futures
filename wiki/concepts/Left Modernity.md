# Left Modernity

## Definition

The project of reclaiming modernity — with its commitments to progress, universalism, reason, and emancipation — as contested terrain for the left, rather than ceding it to neoliberalism or abandoning it to postmodern critique. Articulated by Nick Srnicek and Alex Williams in *[[Inventing the Future - Srnicek Williams|Inventing the Future]]* (Ch.4) as the philosophical grounding for their post-work political programme.

The starting provocation: "modernisation" has become synonymous with privatisation, exploitation, and inequality — the left has ceded the future to the right. The postmodern left, influenced by Lyotard's suspicion of grand narratives, rejected modernity entirely. But this capitulates to neoliberal common sense rather than contesting it. Modernity must be contested, not abandoned.

## Three Pillars

### 1. Hyperstitional Progress

Progress is neither pre-given (the teleological necessity of Enlightenment or orthodox Marxism) nor illusory (the postmodern abandonment of the idea). Progress is **[[Hyperstition|hyperstitional]]**: a fiction that aims to transform itself into truth by catalysing dispersed sentiment into historical force.

This means:
- No predetermined pathway or necessary endpoint
- No guarantee of success — regression is always possible
- Progress must be *constructed* through political struggle, not waited for
- Visions of progress are indispensable because they orient action and generate criteria for adjudicating which struggles to support

The left once excelled at building "enticing visions of a better world" — Soviet cosmic communism, the welfare state's promise of universal provision, the social democratic "white heat of technology." Under neoliberalism, this capacity has atrophied. Left modernity requires rebuilding it.

### 2. Subversive Universalism

Any politics adequate to global capitalism must operate with a universal horizon — capitalism is an expansionary universal and particularisms cannot compete with it. But the Eurocentric universalism of colonial modernity (in which "Europe" occupies the universal position) must be rejected.

The alternative: universals are **empty placeholders** temporarily occupied by hegemonic particulars through political struggle. Key features:
- **Incomplete**: No universal is ever fully achieved; all are open to contestation
- **Subversive**: Because the universal makes unconditional demands, it always exceeds any particular embodiment — enabling struggles to expose who is excluded and demand inclusion ("universalism from below")
- **Heterogeneous**: Does not require homogeneity; capitalism itself accommodates vast cultural difference under a single universalism, and the left's universalism must be similarly pluralistic
- **Co-produced**: A genuinely planetary universalism requires the voices of those outside Europe in its construction

Historical examples: Feminist movements using the universal claim that "all humans are equal" to expose its exclusion of women; anti-colonial movements mobilising universal rights claims against European empires.

### 3. Synthetic Freedom

Against capitalism's **negative freedom** (formal absence of interference), left modernity advocates **[[Synthetic Freedom]]**: constructed freedom of capacities — built through income, time, education, collective resources, and technology. Formal rights without material capacity are hollow.

Underlying this is a rejection of any fixed human nature: "humanity is an incomplete vector of transformation." Emancipation is not liberation of a pre-existing authentic self but the ongoing construction of expanded capacities — theoretical and practical, individual and collective. This open-ended, constructivist humanism connects to [[Xenofeminism]]'s anti-naturalism.

## Against Postmodernism

Left modernity is explicitly a response to the *postmodern* turn in left theory. Lyotard's diagnosis of postmodernity as "incredulity toward metanarratives" was accurate as cultural description — but its political effects have been disastrous for the left:

- The *right* never abandoned grand narratives (neoliberalism is nothing if not a metanarrative)
- "Grand narrative" became code for left progress visions; the right's grand narrative survived
- The result: capitalism appears to have a history with a direction (toward markets, wage labour, commodities), while the left has only local resistances

Postmodern anti-universalism also inadvertently reinscribes Orientalism: if only the West has reason, science, and progress, and all other cultures are irreducible particulars, then non-Western cultures are exoticised rather than liberated.

## Relation to Fisher

Fisher shares the left-modernist diagnosis: [[Capitalist Realism]] and [[Lost Futures]] both describe the cancellation of the left's future-orientation. But Fisher's response was primarily cultural — recovering suppressed future-images through [[Hauntology]] and [[Acid Communism]]. Srnicek/Williams are more programmatic: they propose a political-strategic response (building [[Counter-Hegemony]]) where Fisher offered cultural critique.

The two projects are complementary: Fisher's attention to affect, desire, and cultural possibility maps the psychic terrain that Srnicek/Williams's platform needs to occupy.
